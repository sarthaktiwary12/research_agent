## Coverage Assessment

### Research Question 1: "Does creatine supplementation improve cognitive performance in healthy adults?"
- **Coverage:** Fully Addressed
- **Evidence Quality:** Strong (mostly Silver; includes 3 meta-analyses, largest RCT, and EFSA regulatory evaluation)
- **Number of Sources:** 15+ directly relevant
- **Key Findings:** The evidence converges on a nuanced answer: meta-analyses find small statistically significant effects on memory (SMD ~0.29), but the largest and most rigorous RCT (Sandkuhler 2023, N=123) found no significant effect in preregistered frequentist analysis. Bayesian analysis suggests any true effect is trivially small (d = 0.09-0.17). EFSA rejected the health claim. Young healthy non-stressed adults consistently show null results. Positive findings cluster in older adults and stressed populations.
- **What's Missing:** No studies directly comparing creatine-cognition effects across rested vs. stressed conditions within the same well-powered study. Marshall et al. 2026 (aging-specific review) was noted but not captured as a separate paper.

### Research Question 2: "Which cognitive domains (memory, executive function, processing speed, attention) are most affected?"
- **Coverage:** Fully Addressed
- **Evidence Quality:** Strong (Silver-tier meta-analyses with domain-specific subgroup analyses)
- **Number of Sources:** 10+ directly relevant
- **Key Findings:** Memory is the most consistently supported domain (SMD = 0.29 in Prokopidis meta-analysis), but the effect is driven by older adults (SMD = 0.88 in 66-76 year olds vs. 0.03 in younger adults). Executive function, attention, and processing speed show conflicting or null results. Under stress (sleep deprivation), prefrontal-dependent tasks show improvement. Most other domains are classified as "conflicting" by two independent systematic reviews.
- **What's Missing:** Limited evidence on long-term memory, spatial memory, and verbal fluency specifically. No standardized cognitive battery across studies makes domain-specific conclusions fragile.

### Research Question 3: "What dosing protocols have been studied, and do they differ in effectiveness?"
- **Coverage:** Partially Addressed
- **Evidence Quality:** Moderate (mix of Silver and Bronze; specific dose-response data limited)
- **Number of Sources:** 8 relevant papers
- **Key Findings:** Protocols range from single high dose (0.35 g/kg) to 20 g/day for 8 weeks. EFSA found working memory effects only at 20 g/day for 5-7 days (acute loading), not at lower doses. Forbes dose-response study found null results at both 10 and 20 g/day in young adults. Meta-regression found no significant dose-response. Duration (5 days to 24 weeks) did not moderate effects in meta-analyses. BBB transport is slow, requiring extended supplementation for brain creatine loading (minimum ~1 week). Brain creatine increase is modest (~5-11%).
- **What's Missing:** No systematic comparison of loading vs. maintenance protocols for cognitive outcomes. The optimal dose for brain creatine augmentation is not established. GAA (guanidinoacetic acid) as an alternative delivery agent is mentioned but not deeply explored. Babakhani et al. 2025 (CrM 10g/d + GAA) was identified but not captured as a separate paper. The CONCRET-MENOPA 2025 RCT was noted but not saved.

### Research Question 4: "What are the proposed neurobiological mechanisms by which creatine could enhance cognition?"
- **Coverage:** Fully Addressed
- **Evidence Quality:** Strong (2 Gold-tier claims for established biochemistry; rich mechanistic detail from multiple sources)
- **Number of Sources:** 8+ directly relevant (including Beard & Braissant 2010, Forbes 2023 narrative review, Prokopidis 2022, paper_041 neurotransmitter study)
- **Key Findings:** Three main mechanisms identified: (1) CK-PCr energy buffering system (Gold-tier, well-established); (2) limited BBB transport via SLC6A8 explains modest brain creatine increases (~5-11%); (3) novel neurotransmitter hypothesis — creatine found in synaptic vesicles with stimulation-dependent release and inhibitory effects on pyramidal neurons (preliminary, mouse data). Additional pathways include neuroprotection via oxidative stress modulation, NMDA receptor interaction, and Na+-K+-ATPase interaction. The key mechanistic disconnect is that increased brain creatine does not reliably translate to cognitive improvement in young healthy adults.
- **What's Missing:** The neurotransmitter hypothesis (paper_041) is from a single research group and needs human validation. The specific postsynaptic receptor for creatine is unidentified. No studies measure real-time brain energy metabolism during cognitive tasks in supplemented vs. unsupplemented individuals.

### Research Question 5: "Are there safety concerns or adverse effects of creatine supplementation on brain function?"
- **Coverage:** Minimally Addressed
- **Evidence Quality:** Weak (mostly Bronze; limited systematic safety data for cognitive-dose protocols)
- **Number of Sources:** 3-4 with relevant safety data
- **Key Findings:** Sandkuhler 2023 found side effects 4.25x more frequent with creatine vs. placebo (p = 0.002), though specific side effects not detailed. Smith 2025 pilot reported "acceptable" safety at 20 g/day for 8 weeks in Alzheimer's patients. EFSA evaluation focused on efficacy, not safety. Paper_047 (GI tolerability preprint) noted that higher doses cause more side effects, raising compliance concerns. The general creatine safety literature (for muscular performance) was not deeply explored for brain-specific outcomes.
- **What's Missing:** No systematic review of adverse effects specifically at cognitive-relevant doses (10-20 g/day). No data on long-term brain safety of high-dose creatine. No assessment of kidney function or other organ effects at cognitive dosing levels. The Kreider & Stout review likely discusses safety but only abstract was available. No specific neurological adverse event data (headaches, sleep disturbance, etc.) was captured.
