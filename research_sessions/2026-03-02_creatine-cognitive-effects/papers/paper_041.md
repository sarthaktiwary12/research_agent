## Abstract
Researchers reproducibly detected creatine (Cr) in synaptic vesicles (SVs). Creatine has long been considered as an energy buffer in muscle and brain. This study provides evidence for creatine's presence in synaptic vesicles, its release upon stimulation, and its effects on cortical neurons — suggesting it may function as a central neurotransmitter. This is a fundamentally new perspective on creatine's role in the brain beyond its established function in the phosphocreatine/creatine kinase energy buffering system.

## Deep Reading Notes

> **Note: Analysis based on abstract only. Evidence confidence is reduced.**

### Key Findings
- Creatine (Cr) was reproducibly detected in synaptic vesicles (SVs), at levels lower than glutamate (Glu) and GABA but higher than acetylcholine (ACh) and serotonin (5-HT)
- Calcium-dependent release of creatine was detected when neurons were depolarized by elevated extracellular potassium (K⁺), paralleling the release pattern of classical neurotransmitters Glu and GABA
- Genetic knockout of SLC6A8 (creatine transporter) or AGAT (arginine-glycine amidinotransferase, a creatine synthesis enzyme) significantly decreased both creatine levels in SVs and creatine released upon stimulation
- Creatine at ~100 µM (a concentration comparable to classical neurotransmitters) inhibited approximately one-third of pyramidal neurons in specific cortical regions of the mouse brain
- SLC6A8 was necessary for creatine uptake into synaptosomes, and creatine uptake into SVs occurred in an ATP-dependent manner
- These findings collectively satisfy several classical neurotransmitter criteria: vesicular storage, stimulation-dependent release, postsynaptic action, and reuptake mechanism

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:** The evidence "suggesting" creatine as a neurotransmitter has "not yet reached the level of proof" established for classic transmitters like glutamate, GABA, acetylcholine, and serotonin. This is an important self-qualification.
- **Additional concerns:**
  - This is a preprint (though later published in eLife after peer review), meaning the initial version lacked full peer review
  - All experiments are in mouse models; translatability to human brain function is not established
  - Only ~1/3 of pyramidal neurons responded to creatine, suggesting regional or cell-type specificity that is not yet well characterized
  - The inhibitory action of creatine on pyramidal neurons is interesting but the postsynaptic receptor has not been identified — a key gap for neurotransmitter classification
  - No specific cognitive or behavioral outcomes were measured; all evidence is at the cellular/molecular level
  - The concentration used (100 µM) may or may not be physiologically relevant in the synaptic cleft during normal brain function
  - No discussion of how exogenous creatine supplementation might relate to this neurotransmitter function

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:** Research question 4 (proposed neurobiological mechanisms by which creatine could enhance cognition). If creatine functions as a neurotransmitter, this represents a fundamentally different mechanism than the established phosphocreatine energy-buffering hypothesis.
- **Partially addresses:** Research question 1 (does creatine supplementation improve cognitive performance) — by providing a novel mechanistic basis that could explain cognitive effects, though no direct cognitive data is presented
- **Novel contribution:** This paper offers a paradigm-shifting perspective: creatine may not only serve as an energy buffer in the brain but also as a bona fide neurotransmitter. This is the most mechanistically novel paper in the session, as most other papers focus on the bioenergetic rationale. If confirmed, the neurotransmitter hypothesis could fundamentally change how we understand creatine's cognitive effects and has implications for SLC6A8-deficiency-related intellectual disability.
