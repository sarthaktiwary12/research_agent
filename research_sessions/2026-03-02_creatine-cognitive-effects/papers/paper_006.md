# Creatine monohydrate pilot in Alzheimer's: Feasibility, brain creatine, and cognition

## Abstract
This pilot study is the first to examine the effects of creatine monohydrate supplementation on brain creatine levels and cognition in people with Alzheimer's disease or dementia. The study used MRS to measure brain creatine changes and assessed cognitive outcomes after 8 weeks of supplementation.

## Key Findings

### Study Design
- Pilot feasibility study (first of its kind in Alzheimer's/dementia patients)
- 8 weeks of creatine monohydrate supplementation at 20 g/day
- MRS used to measure brain total creatine (tCr) levels
- Cognitive assessments including working memory and executive function

### Brain Creatine Results (MRS)
- Brain total creatine (tCr) concentration increased in **85% of participants**
- Overall average tCr increase of **11%** after 8 weeks
- Magnitude of change was variable among participants
- Variability may be partially explained by variations in creatine transport kinetics across the blood-brain barrier
- Although previous studies demonstrated brain Cr increases in healthy individuals, this was the **first demonstration in Alzheimer's disease**

### Cognitive Outcomes
- The supplements appeared to produce **moderate improvements in working memory and executive function**
- Results are preliminary given the pilot nature of the study and small sample size

### Feasibility and Safety
- The supplementation protocol was feasible for Alzheimer's patients
- Safety data were acceptable (specific adverse events not detailed in the search summary)

### Significance
- First study to look at effects of creatine on people with Alzheimer's or dementia
- Demonstrates that oral creatine supplementation can increase brain creatine levels even in a neurodegenerative disease context
- Provides proof-of-concept for larger trials

### MRS Methodology Notes
- Used combined ¹H/³¹P MRS methodology
- Future trials recommended to measure both tCr and PCr to elucidate effects on both storage and utilization of energy
- Important to understand dynamics of bioenergetics, not just static levels

## Relation to BBB Challenge
- The challenge of creatine crossing the blood-brain barrier is acknowledged
- The creatine transporter (CT1/SLC6A8) protein is not abundant in BBB capillaries
- The 11% average increase, while meaningful, shows the limitations of oral delivery
- GAA (guanidinoacetic acid), a creatine precursor, has shown up to 16.2% brain creatine increase in healthy men (28 days, 3 g/day) — potentially more BBB-permeable

## Deep Reading Notes

> **Note: Analysis based on abstract only. Evidence confidence is reduced.**

### Key Findings
- Oral creatine monohydrate supplementation (20 g/day for 8 weeks) increased brain total creatine (tCr) levels in 85% of participants with Alzheimer's disease or dementia
- Average brain tCr increase was 11%, as measured by combined 1H/31P MRS
- This is the first study to demonstrate that oral creatine supplementation can increase brain creatine levels in an Alzheimer's disease population (prior MRS work was limited to healthy adults)
- Moderate improvements in working memory and executive function were reported, though specific effect sizes and p-values are not available from the abstract
- The supplementation protocol was deemed feasible and safe for Alzheimer's patients (specific adverse event data not available)
- Considerable inter-individual variability in brain creatine uptake was observed, potentially attributable to differences in creatine transporter (CT1/SLC6A8) kinetics at the blood-brain barrier
- The 11% brain creatine increase is consistent with the ~10% increase reported in healthy adults (see Kreider & Stout 2021), suggesting BBB transport limitations are comparable in healthy and AD populations

### Methodology
- **Study Type:** Pilot feasibility study (open-label, single-arm — no placebo control indicated)
- **Sample Size:** Not explicitly stated in available summary; described as "pilot" with small sample
- **Population/Model:** Adults with Alzheimer's disease or dementia
- **Key Methods:** Combined 1H/31P MRS for brain creatine measurement; standardized cognitive assessments (specific instruments not named in available abstract)
- **Statistical Methods:** Not fully assessable from abstract
- **Duration:** 8 weeks of supplementation at 20 g/day

### Evidence Strength Assessment
- **Tier:** Bronze
- **Reasoning:** This is a pilot feasibility study with no control group mentioned, a small (unstated) sample size, and limited statistical reporting. As a pilot, it is designed to assess feasibility and generate preliminary data rather than test efficacy. The open-label design introduces substantial bias risk. The novelty of being the first creatine study in AD is significant, but the evidence is too preliminary for anything above Bronze. (Would be Silver if it had a control group and larger N; further reduced from abstract-only access.)
- **Risk of Bias:** High — no control group mentioned, open-label design, small sample, limited statistical detail available, potential selection bias in recruitment of AD patients willing to take 20 g/day creatine

   - **Confidence:** Medium (reasonable methodological recommendation; the value of combined MRS is accepted in the field)
   - **Section/Page:** MRS Methodology Notes

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:** Inter-individual variability in brain creatine uptake; limitations of oral delivery given BBB transport constraints; pilot nature requiring confirmation in larger trials
- **Additional concerns:**
  - No control group mentioned — all observed changes could reflect measurement variability, placebo effects, or natural disease fluctuation
  - Sample size is unstated but described as "pilot," likely N < 20, making cognitive outcome assessments underpowered
  - 8 weeks is relatively short for assessing meaningful cognitive change in Alzheimer's disease, where decline is gradual
  - The 20 g/day dose is a loading dose typically used short-term in athletic contexts; long-term tolerability in elderly AD patients is unknown
  - Cognitive assessment instruments are not named, making it impossible to judge their validity for this population
  - No mention of APOE genotype stratification, which could influence both disease progression and metabolic response
  - The study population (AD/dementia) falls outside the primary scope of the research brief, which focuses on healthy adults

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:** Does not directly address the core questions, as the research brief focuses on healthy adults, and this study examines Alzheimer's patients
- **Partially addresses:**
  - Research Question 4 (neurobiological mechanisms): Demonstrates that oral creatine can cross the BBB and increase brain creatine even in neurodegeneration, supporting the mechanistic basis for creatine's potential cognitive effects
  - Research Question 3 (dosing protocols): Provides data on 20 g/day for 8 weeks, relevant for understanding dose-response in brain creatine augmentation
  - Research Question 5 (safety): Preliminary safety data in a vulnerable population
- **Novel contribution:** This is the only study in the session examining creatine supplementation specifically in an Alzheimer's/dementia population. It provides a unique clinical translational perspective, demonstrating that the brain creatine augmentation seen in healthy adults (paper_002, paper_005) also occurs in a neurodegenerative context. This bridges basic mechanistic work and clinical application.
