## Abstract
This pilot study is the first to examine the effects of creatine monohydrate supplementation on brain creatine levels and cognition in people with Alzheimer's disease or dementia. The study used MRS to measure brain creatine changes and assessed cognitive outcomes after 8 weeks of supplementation.

## Key Findings

### Study Design
- Pilot feasibility study (first of its kind in Alzheimer's/dementia patients)
- 8 weeks of creatine monohydrate supplementation at 20 g/day
- MRS used to measure brain total creatine (tCr) levels
- Cognitive assessments including working memory and executive function

### Brain Creatine Results (MRS)
- Brain total creatine (tCr) concentration increased in **85% of participants**
- Overall average tCr increase of **11%** after 8 weeks
- Magnitude of change was variable among participants
- Variability may be partially explained by variations in creatine transport kinetics across the blood-brain barrier
- Although previous studies demonstrated brain Cr increases in healthy individuals, this was the **first demonstration in Alzheimer's disease**

### Cognitive Outcomes
- The supplements appeared to produce **moderate improvements in working memory and executive function**
- Results are preliminary given the pilot nature of the study and small sample size

### Feasibility and Safety
- The supplementation protocol was feasible for Alzheimer's patients
- Safety data were acceptable (specific adverse events not detailed in the search summary)

### Significance
- First study to look at effects of creatine on people with Alzheimer's or dementia
- Demonstrates that oral creatine supplementation can increase brain creatine levels even in a neurodegenerative disease context
- Provides proof-of-concept for larger trials

### MRS Methodology Notes
- Used combined ¹H/³¹P MRS methodology
- Future trials recommended to measure both tCr and PCr to elucidate effects on both storage and utilization of energy
- Important to understand dynamics of bioenergetics, not just static levels

## Relation to BBB Challenge
- The challenge of creatine crossing the blood-brain barrier is acknowledged
- The creatine transporter (CT1/SLC6A8) protein is not abundant in BBB capillaries
- The 11% average increase, while meaningful, shows the limitations of oral delivery
- GAA (guanidinoacetic acid), a creatine precursor, has shown up to 16.2% brain creatine increase in healthy men (28 days, 3 g/day) — potentially more BBB-permeable
