# The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis

## Abstract
This systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to evaluate the effects of creatine monohydrate supplementation on cognitive function in adults and explore its potential role in preventing and delaying cognitive impairment-related diseases. Following the PRISMA 2020 guidelines, a systematic review with meta-analysis was conducted, retrieving RCTs published between 1993 and 2024 from PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science databases. Sixteen RCTs involving 492 participants aged 20.8-76.4 years, including healthy individuals and patients with specific diseases, were selected. The impact of creatine supplementation on overall cognitive function, memory, executive function, attention, and information processing speed was assessed using standardized mean differences (SMD) and Hedge's g with 95% confidence intervals (CI).

## Key Findings
- **Memory:** Significant positive effects (SMD = 0.31, 95% CI: 0.18-0.44; Hedges's g = 0.3003, 95% CI: 0.1778-0.4228) — small-to-moderate effect size
- **Attention time:** Significant positive effects (SMD = -0.31, 95% CI: -0.58 to -0.03)
- **Processing speed time:** Significant improvement (SMD = -0.51, 95% CI: -1.01 to -0.01); however, a 2025 corrigendum corrected this to SMD = 0.01 (95% CI: -0.38 to 0.40), I² = 0%, indicating NO significant effect
- **Overall cognitive function:** No significant improvement
- **Executive function:** No significant improvement
- **Studies included:** 16 RCTs
- **Participants:** 492 (aged 20.8-76.4 years)
- **Subgroup analyses:** More beneficial in individuals with diseases, aged 18-60 years, and females
- **Duration:** No significant difference between short-term (<4 weeks) and long-term (>=4 weeks) interventions
- **GRADE assessment:** Moderate certainty for memory; low certainty for processing speed, overall cognition, executive function, and attention

## Methodological Concerns
- The EFSA Panel noted that this meta-analysis pools results of multiple related cognitive tests from the same studies (not independent), leading to double-counting of participants and inflated sample sizes
- A 2025 corrigendum corrected translation errors and revised the processing speed finding from significant to non-significant
