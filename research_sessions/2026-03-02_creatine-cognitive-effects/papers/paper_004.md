## Abstract / Summary
The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) NDA Panel assessed a health claim application linking creatine supplementation to improvement in cognitive function, pursuant to Article 13(5) of Regulation (EC) No 1924/2006. This is a regulatory evaluation of the totality of evidence for creatine's cognitive benefits.

## Key Findings

### Acute Effects (High Dose)
- The acute effect of creatine on working memory was observed in two studies at 20 g/day for 5–7 days
- This effect was NOT seen at lower doses (2.2–14 g/day)
- This effect was NOT seen with continuous consumption (5 g/day for 6 weeks following a 5-day loading phase)

### Isolated Findings
- The effect on response inhibition at 20 g/day for 7 days was an isolated finding among 10 intervention studies in healthy individuals
- No effects were observed on other cognitive domains in these studies
- In one study, a significant effect was observed in only 1 out of 17 endpoints tested related to cognition
- The primary outcome in that study was not identified
- Corrections for multiple comparisons were not applied, raising the possibility of a chance finding

### Panel's Conclusion
- The EFSA Panel found the evidence **insufficient** to support a general health claim for creatine and cognitive improvement
- The claim was essentially rejected at the regulatory level
- The inconsistency between high-dose acute effects and chronic lower-dose effects was a key concern

## Significance
This is one of the most authoritative and rigorous evaluations of the creatine-cognition evidence, conducted by an independent regulatory body. The negative conclusion carries substantial weight for the field and underscores the gap between popular claims and regulatory-grade evidence.
