## Abstract
The study examined the effect of creatine supplementation and sleep deprivation, with intermittent moderate-intensity exercise, on cognitive and psychomotor performance, mood state, effort, and salivary concentrations of cortisol and melatonin. Subjects were divided into a creatine supplementation group and a placebo group. They took 5 g of creatine monohydrate (or placebo) four times a day (20 g/day total) for 7 days immediately prior to the experiment. Participants undertook tests examining central executive functioning (random number generation), short-term memory, choice reaction time, balance, mood state and effort at baseline and following 18-, 24-, and 36-hour sleep deprivation with moderate intermittent exercise. Saliva samples were taken prior to each set of tests.

## Key Findings
- **Population:** Healthy young adults (exact N not fully specified in available sources; creatine and placebo groups)
- **Design:** Double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled
- **Dose:** 20 g/day creatine monohydrate (4 x 5 g) for 7 days (loading protocol)
- **Stressor:** 36 hours of continuous wakefulness with intermittent moderate-intensity exercise
- **Results:**
  - For random number generation (RNG; central executive function), there was a significant group x time interaction effect
  - The creatine group performed significantly better (p < 0.01) at the 36-hour time point than at other times
  - The only significant between-group effect was at 36 hours, with the creatine group performing better
  - Group x time ANCOVA with baseline performance as covariate showed creatine group performed significantly better (p < 0.05)
  - Short-term creatine supplementation did not alter cortisol levels following 36-hour sleep deprivation relative to placebo
  - No effect on verbal or short-term memory, alertness, or processing speed under these conditions

## Significance in the Field
This study extended the findings of McMorris et al. (2006) by using a longer sleep deprivation paradigm (36 hours vs. 24 hours) and a higher creatine loading dose (20 g/day for 7 days). The key finding — that creatine selectively improved executive function (central executive/working memory) at the most severe point of sleep deprivation (36 hours) but not earlier — supports the hypothesis that creatine's cognitive benefits emerge specifically when the brain is under severe energetic stress. This is consistent with creatine's role in ATP buffering becoming critical when energy demands exceed normal supply. The dissociation between executive function (improved) and simpler memory/alertness tasks (no effect) further refines the stress-condition hypothesis.

## Connection to the 2024 Gordji-Nejad Study (paper_005)
This 2007 study by McMorris et al. is a direct predecessor to the Gordji-Nejad et al. (2024) single-dose creatine study under sleep deprivation. Both studies found cognitive benefits of creatine under sleep deprivation, but Gordji-Nejad used a single acute dose (0.35 g/kg) rather than a 7-day loading protocol, and demonstrated cerebral phosphocreatine changes via MRS. The convergence of findings across these two studies (separated by 17 years) strengthens the evidence for creatine's cognitive effects under sleep deprivation specifically.

## Methodological Concerns
- Small sample size
- The combined stressor of sleep deprivation + exercise introduces confounds
- Benefits only appeared at 36 hours — this is an extreme condition rarely encountered in real life
- No brain creatine measurement (MRS) to confirm that the 7-day loading protocol increased brain creatine
- The selective finding at only the most extreme time point raises questions about clinical significance
