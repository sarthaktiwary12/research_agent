## Abstract
This review examines the effects of creatine supplementation on brain function and health. Creatine is an essential brain compound critical to energy metabolism — mitochondria in cells convert nutrients and oxygen into energy molecules, and creatine's role is to transport that energy to where it needs to be. Apart from muscle cells, neurons carry out many processes that require energy, and exogenously administered creatine has been shown to increase energy supply to neurons in healthy adults.

## Key Content Areas

### Brain Creatine Metabolism
- Creatine is stored in the body as phosphocreatine (PCr)
- When ATP is used as energy in neurons, it loses a phosphate and becomes ADP
- Creatine donates a phosphate to regenerate ATP from ADP
- Creatine uptake into the CNS is limited by the creatine transporter SLC6A8 (CreatT), which operates near saturation at the blood-brain barrier
- Brain creatine increase from supplementation (~10%) is approximately half that of skeletal muscle (~20%)

### MRS Evidence for Brain Creatine Increases
- Supplementation of 20 g/day for 7–28 days has shown most consistent brain creatine increases in healthy adults
- Three studies found improvements in brain creatine levels; two found no effect
- Large heterogeneity across studies in supplementation duration, MRS methods (proton vs. phosphorus), and brain region assessed

### Cognitive Effects
- Some evidence for benefits to short-term memory and intelligence/reasoning
- Creatine may improve working memory, processing speed, and attention
- Benefits appear most pronounced under stress conditions (sleep deprivation, mental fatigue)
- Vegetarians may benefit more due to lower baseline creatine levels

### Neuroprotection
- Creatine may help prevent excitotoxicity through its role in elevating ATP levels
- Potential applications in traumatic brain injury, concussion, depression
- Two large, rigorous trials in Parkinson's disease found no clinical benefit (one terminated early for futility)

### Clinical Applications
- Promise for attenuating symptoms of concussion, mild traumatic brain injury, and depression
- Effect on neurodegenerative diseases (Parkinson's) appears lacking
- Potential for mood disorders based on brain bioenergetics data
