# The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis

## Abstract
This systematic review and meta-analysis evaluated the effects of creatine monohydrate supplementation on cognitive function in adults and explored its potential role in preventing and delaying cognitive impairment-related diseases. Sixteen randomized controlled trials (RCTs) involving 492 participants aged 20.8–76.4 years were included, encompassing both healthy individuals and patients with specific diseases. The impact of creatine supplementation on overall cognitive function, memory, executive function, attention, and information processing speed was assessed.

## Key Findings
- Creatine supplementation showed significant positive effects on **memory** and **attention time**
- Significant improvement in **processing speed time**
- **No significant improvements** on overall cognitive function or executive function
- Subgroup analyses: creatine was more beneficial in individuals with diseases, those aged 18–60 years, and females
- No significant differences between short-term (<4 weeks) and long-term (≥4 weeks) interventions
- GRADE assessment: moderate certainty for memory; low certainty for processing speed, overall cognitive function, executive function, and attention
- The effects may reach a saturation point in the short term; extending intervention duration does not provide additional benefits

## Conclusions
Current evidence suggests that creatine monohydrate supplementation may confer beneficial effects on cognitive function in adults, particularly in the domains of memory, attention time, and information processing speed. However, larger robust clinical trials are warranted to further validate these findings.

## Deep Reading Notes

> **Note: Analysis based on abstract only. Evidence confidence is reduced.**

### Key Findings
- Creatine monohydrate supplementation produced statistically significant positive effects on **memory** and **attention time** in a pooled meta-analysis of 16 RCTs (N=492)
- Significant improvement in **processing speed time** was also observed
- **No significant improvement** was found for overall cognitive function or executive function when pooled across studies
- Subgroup analyses revealed that creatine was more beneficial in: (a) individuals with diseases vs. healthy individuals, (b) adults aged 18-60 years, and (c) females compared to males
- No significant difference was found between short-term (<4 weeks) and long-term (>=4 weeks) supplementation durations, suggesting a possible saturation effect
- GRADE certainty assessment: moderate for memory; low for processing speed, overall cognitive function, executive function, and attention
- The authors suggest effects may reach a saturation point in the short term, meaning extended intervention duration does not confer additional cognitive benefits

- **Population/Model:** Adults aged 20.8-76.4 years, including both healthy individuals and patients with specific diseases
- **Key Methods:** Systematic literature search, meta-analytic pooling of RCT data, subgroup analyses by health status, age, sex, and intervention duration; GRADE assessment of evidence certainty

### Evidence Strength Assessment
- **Tier:** Silver

The mixed population (healthy + diseased) also complicates interpretation for the specific research question about healthy adults.

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:** The authors acknowledge that "larger robust clinical trials are warranted to further validate these findings," implying recognized limitations in existing evidence
- **Additional concerns:**
  - Total N=492 across 16 RCTs is small for a meta-analysis, averaging ~31 participants per study, indicating many underpowered individual trials
  - Mixing healthy individuals with disease populations in the same meta-analysis complicates the interpretation for our research question specifically about healthy adults
  - The wide age range (20.8-76.4 years) introduces substantial population heterogeneity
  - GRADE ratings of "low certainty" for most outcomes (processing speed, overall cognitive function, executive function, attention) indicate the evidence base is weak
  - Specific dosing protocols used across the 16 included RCTs are not described in the abstract, making it impossible to assess dose-response relationships
  - Without full text access, we cannot evaluate: the specific search strategy, risk of bias in individual studies, forest plots, heterogeneity statistics (I-squared), publication bias assessment, or sensitivity analyses
  - The finding that disease populations benefit more than healthy individuals raises the question of whether the overall positive finding for memory/attention is driven primarily by the disease subgroup
