## Abstract
This systematic review examined whether creatine supplementation research supports the theoretical basis for an effect on cognition. The review found that while creatine supplementation can increase brain creatine content, the results for effects on cognition are "somewhat equivocal." Previous narrative reviews have only provided limited support for cognitive benefit claims.

## Key Findings

### Brain Creatine Content
- The review supported claims that creatine supplementation can increase brain creatine content (confirmed by MRS studies)
- However, increased brain creatine does not automatically translate to improved cognitive function

### Equivocal Cognitive Results
- Evidence for short-term memory and intelligence/reasoning improvements exists but is inconsistent
- Results for long-term memory, spatial memory, attention, executive function, response inhibition, word fluency, reaction time, and mental fatigue were conflicting
- No significant improvements in overall cognitive function or executive function in meta-analyses

### Methodological Concerns
- Limited sample sizes across existing studies
- Inconsistent findings across different cognitive domains
- Heterogeneity in baseline characteristics (age, sex, diet)
- Differing supplementation protocols
- Varying cognitive tasks and assessment tools
- Small total sample sizes (e.g., 281 participants across 6 studies in one prior review)
- Issues with supplementation regimens need to be addressed

### Disconnect Between Theory and Evidence
- The theoretical mechanism (increased brain energy availability via ATP recycling) is plausible
- However, the empirical evidence does not consistently support the predicted cognitive improvements
- The review concludes that current research fails to robustly support the theoretical basis for creatine's effect on cognition
