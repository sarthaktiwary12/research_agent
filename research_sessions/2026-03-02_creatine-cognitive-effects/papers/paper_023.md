# The effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive performance — a randomised controlled study

## Abstract
This is the largest randomized controlled trial to date examining the cognitive effects of creatine supplementation in healthy adults. The study was designed to replicate earlier findings suggesting cognitive benefits from creatine supplementation. Participants received either creatine or placebo and were assessed on multiple cognitive tasks including Raven's Advanced Progressive Matrices (RAPM) and Backward Digit Span (BDS).

## Key Findings
- **Overall result:** There was no indication that creatine improved the performance of exploratory cognitive tasks
- **Bayesian analysis:** Found evidence for a small beneficial effect of creatine on cognition for both tasks (RAPM and BDS)
- **Effect sizes:** Cohen's d = 0.09 for RAPM and 0.17 for BDS (very small effects)
- **IQ equivalent:** If these were IQ tests, the increase would correspond to 1 and 2.5 IQ points respectively
- **Frequentist analysis (preregistered):** No significant effect at p < 0.05 (two-tailed), though BDS bordered significance
- **Moderators:** No influence of diet (vegetarian vs omnivore), age, or sex on this effect
- **Distribution of p-values:** Consistent with what would be expected if there was no true effect
- **Conclusion on brain creatine:** Evidence from RCTs and brain creatine studies does not support the idea that dietary creatine affects brain health

## Study Design
- **Design:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled
- **Population:** Healthy adults
- **Cognitive tasks:** Raven's Advanced Progressive Matrices (RAPM), Backward Digit Span (BDS)
- **Sample size:** Largest study on cognitive effects of creatine to date (exact N not retrieved; described as the largest)

## Main Conclusions
This study represents the most well-powered RCT on creatine and cognition to date. The preregistered frequentist analysis found no significant effect. While Bayesian analysis suggested a small positive effect, the effect sizes were trivially small (Cohen's d = 0.09-0.17). The authors concluded that there was no indication that creatine improved cognitive performance, and that the distribution of p-values was consistent with a null effect. This is a critical "null result" paper that tempers the positive findings from meta-analyses.

### Key Findings
- In the preregistered frequentist analysis, creatine supplementation (5 g/day for 6 weeks) did **not** significantly improve performance on either primary cognitive outcome: Backward Digit Span (BDS, p = 0.064, partial eta-squared = 0.029) or Raven's Advanced Progressive Matrices (RAPM, p = 0.327, partial eta-squared = 0.008)
- Bayesian analysis found weak evidence in favor of a **small** beneficial creatine effect and **strong** evidence against a large creatine effect (Bayes factors compared point null to point alternative models with effect sizes d = 0.04–0.17)
- Effect sizes were trivially small: Cohen's d = 0.09 for RAPM and d = 0.17 for BDS, corresponding to approximately 1 and 2.5 IQ points respectively if these were IQ tests
- No indication that creatine improved performance on any of the eight exploratory cognitive tasks (covering attention, verbal fluency, task switching, memory)
- Vegetarians did **not** benefit more from creatine than omnivores — no moderating effect of diet
- No moderating effects of age or sex were detected
- Side effects were reported significantly more often in the creatine group than placebo (p = 0.002, RR = 4.25)
- The distribution of p-values across outcomes was consistent with what would be expected under a true null effect

### Methodology
- **Study Type:** Preregistered, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, crossover trial (RCT)
- **Sample Size:** N = 123 healthy adults (approximately half vegetarian, half omnivore); the largest study of creatine and cognition to date at time of publication
- **Population/Model:** Healthy adults, balanced for vegetarian/omnivore dietary status
- **Key Methods:** Two primary cognitive tasks (RAPM, BDS) plus eight exploratory cognitive tasks; crossover design with each participant receiving both creatine and placebo in counterbalanced order; 5 g/day creatine monohydrate for 6 weeks per arm
- **Statistical Methods:** Preregistered frequentist analysis (two-tailed, alpha = 0.05) as primary; Bayesian analysis as secondary (point null vs. point alternative models at multiple effect sizes; also half-normal prior distributions); partial eta-squared for variance explained; relative risk for side effect analysis
- **Duration:** 6 weeks per arm in crossover design (total study duration not specified; washout period duration between arms not reported in available sources)

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:**
  - Despite being the largest study to date, sample size may still be insufficient to detect very small but potentially meaningful effects
  - 4% of data could not be analyzed due to labeling errors (participant/timepoint)
  - COVID-19 pandemic started mid-study, adding noise and necessitating a switch from in-person to video-based cognitive testing
  - Washout period details between crossover arms not specified in available sources
- **Additional concerns:**
  - Only two cognitive domains were confirmatory (RAPM, BDS); the remaining eight were exploratory, meaning the study may not have been powered for those outcomes
  - The tension between Bayesian results (small positive effect) and frequentist results (non-significant) is not fully resolved — the study simultaneously argues both for and against an effect depending on analytic framework
  - Crossover design assumes no carryover effect, but brain creatine stores may take weeks to return to baseline after supplementation ceases — if washout was inadequate, this could bias results toward the null
  - The 6-week supplementation period may not be long enough to fully saturate brain creatine stores, as brain uptake is slower than muscle uptake
  - Testing via video call during COVID could have introduced measurement variability not present in in-person testing

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:** Questions 1 (Does creatine improve cognitive performance in healthy adults? — provides the strongest null-leaning evidence), Question 2 (Which cognitive domains are affected? — tested multiple domains including reasoning, working memory, attention, verbal fluency, task switching), Question 3 (Dosing protocols — used 5 g/day for 6 weeks)
- **Partially addresses:** Question 5 (Safety — reports significantly higher side effects with creatine, RR = 4.25)

Its null-leaning result with Bayesian nuance (tiny effect possible but large effects ruled out) is essential for any balanced assessment of the evidence base
