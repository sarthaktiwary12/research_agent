## Abstract
This review comprehensively examines the synthesis and transport of creatine in the central nervous system and its importance for cerebral functions. Creatine is taken up by the CNS from the periphery through the creatine transporter SLC6A8 at the blood-brain barrier (BBB), but in limited amounts. While SLC6A8 is expressed by microcapillary endothelial cells (MCEC) at the BBB, it is absent from astrocytes, particularly their feet lining microcapillary endothelial cells. This implies that only low amounts of peripheral creatine can enter the brain through the limited endothelial surface free of astrocytic feet, and that the CNS must also ensure its own endogenous synthesis of creatine. The enzymes AGAT and GAMT are expressed in the CNS, enabling brain creatine synthesis. There is strong indication that creatine in the brain is mainly synthesized by glial cells and transferred to neurons via SLC6A8, which may explain why hereditary transporter deficiency (caused by SLC6A8 mutations) results in severe brain damage despite the possibility of endogenous synthesis. Reinforcing the importance of SLC6A8 in brain creatine supply, no detectable creatine signal was found in the brains of patients with SLC6A8 mutations. The review discusses how CNS uptake of exogenous creatine from the periphery is marginal and takes considerable time, explaining why most studies require minimum supplementation periods of one week or longer.
