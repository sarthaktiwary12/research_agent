## Abstract
Creatine supplementation is in widespread use to enhance sports-fitness performance, and has been trialled successfully in the treatment of neurological, neuromuscular and atherosclerotic disease. Creatine plays a pivotal role in brain energy homeostasis, being a temporal and spatial buffer for cytosolic and mitochondrial pools of the cellular energy currency, adenosine triphosphate and its regulator, adenosine diphosphate. The authors tested the hypothesis that oral creatine supplementation (5 g per day for six weeks) would enhance intelligence test scores and working memory performance in 45 young adult, vegetarian subjects in a double-blind, placebo-controlled, cross-over design. Creatine supplementation had a significant positive effect (p < 0.0001) on both working memory (backward digit span) and intelligence (Raven's Advanced Progressive Matrices), both tasks that require speed of processing.

## Key Findings
- **Population:** 45 young adult vegetarians/vegans (chosen because omnivores obtain variable dietary creatine)
- **Design:** Double-blind, placebo-controlled, cross-over
- **Dose:** 5 g/day creatine monohydrate for 6 weeks per arm
- **Cognitive tests:** Backward Digit Span (working memory) and Raven's Advanced Progressive Matrices (intelligence/reasoning)
- **Results:** Highly significant improvement on both tasks (p < 0.0001)
- **Working memory:** Digit span improved from approximately 7 to 8.5 digits
- **Effect size:** Large (estimated d ~1.0 based on subsequent meta-analytic classification); the largest effect ever reported in creatine-cognition literature
- **Interpretation:** Findings underline a dynamic and significant role of brain energy capacity in influencing brain performance

## Significance in the Field
This is the foundational study that launched the modern investigation of creatine as a cognitive enhancer. It has been cited approximately 350+ times and was the specific study that Sandkühler et al. (2023, paper_023) attempted to replicate. The Sandkühler replication found only trivially small effects (d = 0.09-0.17), with the Bayesian analysis strongly disfavoring the large effect size (~d = 1.0) reported by Rae et al. This discrepancy is a central tension in the field.

## Methodological Concerns Raised by Subsequent Literature
- Small sample size (N=45) in a cross-over design
- Exclusive use of vegetarian participants limits generalizability to the general population
- The very large effect size (d ~1.0) has not been replicated in any subsequent study
- No brain creatine measurement (MRS) to confirm that supplementation actually increased brain creatine levels
- The 2023 replication by Sandkühler et al. (N=123, including both vegetarians and omnivores) found no significant effect in the preregistered frequentist analysis
