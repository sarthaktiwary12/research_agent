# Effects of creatine supplementation on memory in healthy individuals: a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials

## Abstract
This systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials was conducted to determine the effects of creatine supplementation on memory performance in healthy humans. Ten RCTs examining the effect of creatine supplementation compared with placebo on measures of memory in healthy individuals met the inclusion criteria for systematic review, 8 of which were included in the meta-analysis.

## Key Findings
- **Overall memory effect:** Creatine supplementation improved measures of memory compared with placebo (SMD = 0.29, 95% CI: 0.04-0.53; I² = 66%; P = 0.02)
- **Older adults (66-76 years):** Significant improvement (SMD = 0.88; 95% CI: 0.22-1.55; I² = 83%; P = 0.009) — large effect size
- **Younger participants (11-31 years):** No significant improvement (SMD = 0.03; 95% CI: -0.14 to 0.20; I² = 0%; P = 0.72) — essentially null effect
- **Dose:** Creatine dose (~2.2-20 g/d) did not influence findings
- **Duration:** Intervention duration (5 days to 24 weeks) did not influence findings
- **Sex and geography:** Did not influence findings
- **Studies included:** 10 (8 in meta-analysis)
- **Focus:** Memory only, healthy individuals

## Methodological Concerns
- High heterogeneity in overall analysis (I² = 66%) and older adult subgroup (I² = 83%)
- A published critique noted that "double-counting due to inadequate statistics leads to false-positive findings"
- Small sample sizes across included studies

## Main Conclusions
Creatine supplementation enhanced measures of memory performance in healthy individuals, with the benefit primarily driven by large effects in older adults (66-76 years). Younger adults showed essentially no benefit. The age-dependent effect may relate to age-associated declines in muscle and brain creatine levels, reduced physical activity, and lower dietary creatine intake in older populations.

## Deep Reading Notes

> **Note: Analysis based on abstract only. Evidence confidence is reduced.**

### Key Findings
- Creatine supplementation produced a small but statistically significant improvement in memory compared with placebo across pooled RCTs (SMD = 0.29, 95% CI: 0.04-0.53; P = 0.02), though heterogeneity was substantial (I² = 66%)
- The effect was strongly age-dependent: older adults (66-76 years) showed a large effect size (SMD = 0.88, 95% CI: 0.22-1.55; P = 0.009), while younger participants (11-31 years) showed essentially no effect (SMD = 0.03, 95% CI: -0.14 to 0.20; P = 0.72)
- Dose (ranging from ~2.2-20 g/d), duration (5 days to 24 weeks), sex, and geography did not significantly moderate findings
- The authors propose that the age-dependent benefit may relate to age-associated declines in muscle and brain creatine levels, reduced physical activity, and lower dietary creatine intake in older populations
- A published critique has raised concerns about "double-counting due to inadequate statistics" potentially leading to false-positive findings

### Methodology
- **Study Type:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs
- **Sample Size:** 10 RCTs in systematic review, 8 included in meta-analysis (total N not specified in available abstract)
- **Population/Model:** Healthy individuals; subgroups by age (older adults 66-76 years vs. younger 11-31 years)
- **Key Methods:** Systematic literature search, PRISMA-guided methodology, random-effects meta-analysis, subgroup analyses by age, dose, duration, sex, and geography
- **Statistical Methods:** Standardized mean difference (SMD) with 95% confidence intervals; heterogeneity assessed via I² statistic; significance threshold P < 0.05 (not fully assessable from abstract)
- **Duration:** Included studies ranged from 5 days to 24 weeks of intervention

### Evidence Strength Assessment
- **Tier:** Silver
- **Reasoning:** This is a systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs, which ordinarily qualifies as Gold-tier evidence. However, the tier is reduced to Silver for the following reasons: (1) analysis is based on abstract only; (2) high heterogeneity (I² = 66% overall, 83% in older adult subgroup) suggests substantial inconsistency across studies; (3) a published critique raised concerns about statistical double-counting leading to potential false-positive findings; (4) small number of included studies (10 in review, 8 in meta-analysis) with small sample sizes; (5) the confidence interval for the overall effect barely excludes zero (lower bound 0.04), indicating a fragile result
- **Risk of Bias:** Medium — The meta-analysis itself follows standard methodology, but the high heterogeneity and small study base raise concerns. The published critique about double-counting is a serious methodological concern that could invalidate the positive overall finding. Individual included studies reportedly had small sample sizes, increasing risk of bias and publication bias

### Specific Claims

   - **Evidence:** Pooled meta-analysis of 8 RCTs; statistically significant but small effect size; high heterogeneity (I² = 66%)
   - **Confidence:** Medium — The effect is statistically significant but the lower CI bound (0.04) is close to zero, heterogeneity is high, and a published critique challenges the statistical approach
   - **Section/Page:** Abstract / Key findings

2. **Claim:** Older adults (66-76 years) show significantly greater memory improvement with creatine supplementation (SMD = 0.88, 95% CI: 0.22-1.55; P = 0.009)
   - **Evidence:** Subgroup analysis; large effect size but very high heterogeneity (I² = 83%) and wide confidence interval
   - **Confidence:** Medium — Large and significant effect, but I² = 83% indicates that the included studies vary enormously; the subgroup is likely based on very few studies, limiting reliability
   - **Section/Page:** Abstract / Key findings

3. **Claim:** Younger participants (11-31 years) do not benefit from creatine supplementation for memory (SMD = 0.03, 95% CI: -0.14 to 0.20; P = 0.72)
   - **Evidence:** Subgroup analysis; near-zero effect size with no heterogeneity (I² = 0%), consistently null result
   - **Confidence:** Medium — The null finding is robust (low heterogeneity, tight CI), though still based on a limited number of studies. Confidence reduced from high due to abstract-only analysis
   - **Section/Page:** Abstract / Key findings

4. **Claim:** Creatine dose (~2.2-20 g/d) and intervention duration (5 days to 24 weeks) did not influence findings
   - **Evidence:** Subgroup or meta-regression analyses (methodology details not fully available from abstract)
   - **Confidence:** Low — This is an important null finding, but the wide range of doses and durations, combined with few total studies, suggests the analysis may have been underpowered to detect dose-response or time-course effects
   - **Section/Page:** Abstract / Key findings

5. **Claim:** The age-dependent effect may relate to age-associated declines in muscle and brain creatine levels, reduced physical activity, and lower dietary creatine intake in older populations
   - **Evidence:** Proposed mechanistic explanation by the authors; not directly tested in the meta-analysis
   - **Confidence:** Low — This is an interpretive hypothesis, not a data-driven finding from the meta-analysis itself
   - **Section/Page:** Main conclusions

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:** Not fully accessible from abstract, but the high heterogeneity values are acknowledged
- **Additional concerns:**
  - The overall positive effect (SMD = 0.29) has a lower CI bound of just 0.04, making it fragile — a single study removal could render it non-significant
  - I² = 83% in the older adult subgroup is very high, suggesting the studies in this subgroup produced widely varying results, undermining confidence in the pooled estimate
  - A published external critique noted "double-counting due to inadequate statistics leads to false-positive findings" — this is a serious concern that could invalidate the primary positive result
  - Focus on memory only limits generalizability to broader cognitive function
  - Small number of included studies (10/8) limits the power of subgroup analyses and the ability to assess publication bias
  - The age range of "younger" participants (11-31) includes adolescents, which may confound interpretation for healthy adults specifically
  - Full text not available for detailed assessment of search strategy, risk of bias evaluation, or sensitivity analyses
