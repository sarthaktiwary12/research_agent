# Single dose creatine improves cognitive performance and induces changes in cerebral high energy phosphates during sleep deprivation

## Abstract
This study investigated whether a single high dose of creatine monohydrate could improve cognitive performance during sleep deprivation and induce changes in cerebral high-energy phosphates. In a randomised, controlled, double-blind crossover trial, 15 healthy subjects were subjected to 21 hours of sleep deprivation. Each participant received a single dose of creatine monohydrate (0.35 g/kg body weight) and a placebo in random order.

## Key Findings

### Study Design
- Randomised, controlled, double-blind crossover trial
- 15 healthy subjects
- 21 hours of sleep deprivation
- Single dose: 0.35 g/kg body weight creatine monohydrate (approximately 24.5 g for a 70 kg person)
- MRS measurements: combined ¹H-MRS and ³¹P-MRS

### Brain Metabolite Results (MRS)
- Brain phosphocreatine (PCr) levels significantly increased after creatine administration
- Creatine was found to induce changes in high-energy brain phosphates
- The changes in brain bioenergetics were detectable even after a single high dose

### Cognitive Performance Results
- Creatine improved cognitive performance during sleep deprivation
- Faster information processing was observed
- Reduction in subjective fatigue compared to placebo
- Creatine alleviated changes in phosphates, pH, and cognitive performance associated with sleep deprivation

### Mechanistic Insights
- The inverse effects of creatine supplementation and sleep deprivation on high-energy phosphates, neural creatine, and cognitive performance suggest that creatine counteracts the metabolic/energetic deficits caused by sleep deprivation
- The hypothesis: high extracellular creatine availability combined with increased intracellular energy consumption during sleep deprivation temporarily increases central creatine uptake

### Novelty
- Revises the established assumption that creatine supplementation only works over a longer period (weeks of loading)
- First demonstration that a single acute dose can produce measurable brain metabolite changes and cognitive effects
- Suggests that demand-dependent uptake (during energy stress) may be a key factor

## Conclusions
Creatine is a suitable candidate for reducing the negative effects of sleep deprivation on cognition. A high single dose can partially reverse metabolic alterations and fatigue-related cognitive deterioration.

## Deep Reading Notes

> **Note: Analysis based on abstract and WebSearch summaries only. Full text could not be retrieved (HTTP 403 from Nature, PMC, and PubMed). Evidence confidence is reduced accordingly.**

### Key Findings
- A single high dose of creatine monohydrate (0.35 g/kg body weight, ~24.5 g for a 70 kg individual) significantly improved cognitive performance during 21 hours of sleep deprivation compared to placebo
- Brain phosphocreatine (PCr) levels significantly increased after creatine administration, as measured by 31P-MRS; creatine also induced changes in PCr/Pi ratio, ATP levels, and tCr/tNAA ratio
- Creatine prevented the sleep-deprivation-induced drop in brain pH, suggesting reduced cellular acidification
- Cognitive improvements included faster information processing, improved short-term memory, and better performance on logical and numerical tasks
- The cognitive effect reached its peak at approximately 4 hours post-administration and lasted up to 9 hours
- Positive effects on brain metabolism and cognitive performance were detectable as early as 3 hours after oral intake
- Reduction in subjective fatigue was observed compared to placebo
- Bonferroni-corrected significance was reported at p <= 0.005 for metabolic changes versus baseline (specific test statistics not available from abstract)

### Methodology
- **Study Type:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover trial (RCT)
- **Sample Size:** N=15 healthy subjects (7 received creatine first, 8 received placebo first)
- **Population/Model:** Healthy adults subjected to 21 hours of sleep deprivation
- **Key Methods:** Combined 1H-MRS and 31P-MRS for brain metabolite measurements; psychomotor vigilance test (PVT); subjective fatigue scores; cognitive task battery (specific tasks for short-term memory, processing speed, logical and numerical reasoning). Measurements were acquired in four runs: baseline at 6 p.m., then at midnight, 2 a.m., and 4 a.m. Each session lasted ~1 hour 35 minutes and comprised two 31P-MRS, three 1H-MRS measurements, followed by fatigue scores, PVT, and cognitive tasks. Creatine or placebo was administered at 8:30 p.m. Washout period of at least 5 days between crossover arms.
- **Statistical Methods:** Bonferroni correction applied for multiple comparisons; significance threshold reported at p <= 0.005. Specific test statistics (F-values, t-values, effect sizes) not available from abstract summaries.
- **Duration:** Single-session acute study (21 hours of sleep deprivation per arm); crossover design with minimum 5-day washout
- **Institution:** Institute of Neuroscience and Medicine (INM-2), Forschungszentrum Jülich, Germany; RWTH Aachen University Hospital; University of Cologne

### Evidence Strength Assessment
- **Tier:** Silver
- **Reasoning:** This is a well-designed RCT with a crossover design (reducing inter-individual variability), double-blinding, placebo control, and Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons — features that would typically warrant a Gold tier. However, the tier is adjusted to Silver for several reasons: (1) very small sample size (N=15); (2) only a single acute session was studied; (3) the stressor condition (sleep deprivation) is specific and may not generalize to rested states; (4) the dose used (0.35 g/kg) is unusually high and not typical of supplementation protocols; (5) abstract-only access limits full assessment of statistical rigor and potential bias. Published in Scientific Reports (Nature portfolio), a reputable but broadly scoped journal.
- **Risk of Bias:** Medium — The crossover design and blinding are strengths. However, with N=15, the study is underpowered to detect small effects, and there is risk of false positives despite corrections. The high acute dose raises questions about gastrointestinal tolerability and potential unblinding (creatine's taste/texture). No information available about adverse event monitoring or success of blinding assessment. The specific condition of sleep deprivation means results may reflect creatine's ability to restore depleted energy rather than enhance cognition per se.

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors (from available summaries):**
  - Small sample size (N=15)
  - Future research needs to investigate the appropriate dose
  - Need to more accurately determine the time point at which creatine reaches its maximum effect
  - The specific condition (sleep deprivation + high dose) limits generalizability
- **Additional concerns:**
  - The dose of 0.35 g/kg (~24.5 g in a single dose) is very high — roughly 3.5x the typical sports loading dose and may cause gastrointestinal distress, which could affect blinding and compliance
  - No information on whether blinding was assessed (creatine and placebo may differ in taste/texture at this dose)
  - Cognitive improvements may reflect restoration of sleep-deprived deficits rather than genuine cognitive enhancement — results should not be extrapolated to well-rested individuals
  - Crossover design with only a 5-day washout; creatine's half-life in brain tissue is not well characterized, and residual effects from the first arm cannot be fully excluded
  - Only 15 subjects limits statistical power and generalizability; demographic details (age range, sex distribution) were not available from abstracts
  - No information on dietary creatine intake or baseline creatine status of participants, which could confound results
  - The study measured only acute effects; long-term implications are unknown
  - Publication in Scientific Reports (while reputable) uses a less selective peer-review process than specialty neuroscience journals
