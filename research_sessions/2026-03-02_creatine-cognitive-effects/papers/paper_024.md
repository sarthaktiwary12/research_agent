# Dose-Response of Creatine Supplementation on Cognitive Function in Healthy Young Adults

## Abstract
This dose-response study examined the effects of two different doses of creatine monohydrate supplementation on cognitive function in healthy young adults. Thirty participants were evenly randomized to receive supplementation with creatine (CR10: 10 g/day or CR20: 20 g/day) or a placebo (PLA: 10 g/day) for 6 weeks. Participants completed a cognitive test battery (processing speed, episodic memory, and attention) on two separate occasions prior to and following supplementation.

## Key Findings
- **Overall result:** Creatine (independent of dosage) had no significant effect on the measures of cognitive performance
- **Processing speed:** No improvement observed at either dose
- **Working memory:** No improvement observed at either dose
- **Executive function:** No improvement observed at either dose
- **Dose comparison:** Neither 10 g/day nor 20 g/day produced cognitive benefits
- **Sample size:** 30 participants (10 per group)
- **Duration:** 6 weeks
- **Population:** Healthy, young adults

## Study Design
- **Design:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, dose-response
- **Groups:** CR10 (10 g/day creatine), CR20 (20 g/day creatine), PLA (placebo)
- **Duration:** 6 weeks
- **Cognitive battery:** Processing speed, episodic memory, attention
- **Population:** Healthy young adults

## Main Conclusions
Contrary to the positive results of previous studies, these findings were unable to demonstrate any positive effects of moderate or high creatine dosage on processing speed, working memory, or executive function performance in healthy young adults. The authors suggested that a more complex set of cognitive tasks or ones that induce greater mental fatigue are needed to prompt additional brain activity in healthy young adults. The authors also noted that whether an increase in brain creatine levels from creatine supplementation leads to downstream improvements in central nervous system and cognitive functioning in young, healthy adults remains to be fully determined.

## Significance
This study is important because it tests a dose-response relationship and finds null results even at high doses (20 g/day) in young adults, suggesting the lack of cognitive benefit is not simply a dosing issue but may reflect a genuine absence of effect in young, healthy, non-stressed populations.
