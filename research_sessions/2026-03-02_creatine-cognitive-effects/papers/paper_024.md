# Dose-Response of Creatine Supplementation on Cognitive Function in Healthy Young Adults

## Abstract
This dose-response study examined the effects of two different doses of creatine monohydrate supplementation on cognitive function in healthy young adults. Thirty participants were evenly randomized to receive supplementation with creatine (CR10: 10 g/day or CR20: 20 g/day) or a placebo (PLA: 10 g/day) for 6 weeks. Participants completed a cognitive test battery (processing speed, episodic memory, and attention) on two separate occasions prior to and following supplementation.

## Key Findings
- **Overall result:** Creatine (independent of dosage) had no significant effect on the measures of cognitive performance
- **Processing speed:** No improvement observed at either dose
- **Working memory:** No improvement observed at either dose
- **Executive function:** No improvement observed at either dose
- **Dose comparison:** Neither 10 g/day nor 20 g/day produced cognitive benefits
- **Sample size:** 30 participants (10 per group)
- **Duration:** 6 weeks
- **Population:** Healthy, young adults

## Study Design
- **Design:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, dose-response
- **Groups:** CR10 (10 g/day creatine), CR20 (20 g/day creatine), PLA (placebo)
- **Duration:** 6 weeks
- **Cognitive battery:** Processing speed, episodic memory, attention
- **Population:** Healthy young adults

## Main Conclusions
Contrary to the positive results of previous studies, these findings were unable to demonstrate any positive effects of moderate or high creatine dosage on processing speed, working memory, or executive function performance in healthy young adults. The authors suggested that a more complex set of cognitive tasks or ones that induce greater mental fatigue are needed to prompt additional brain activity in healthy young adults. The authors also noted that whether an increase in brain creatine levels from creatine supplementation leads to downstream improvements in central nervous system and cognitive functioning in young, healthy adults remains to be fully determined.

## Significance
This study is important because it tests a dose-response relationship and finds null results even at high doses (20 g/day) in young adults, suggesting the lack of cognitive benefit is not simply a dosing issue but may reflect a genuine absence of effect in young, healthy, non-stressed populations.

## Deep Reading Notes

> **Note: Analysis based on abstract only. Evidence confidence is reduced.**

### Key Findings
- Creatine supplementation at either 10 g/day or 20 g/day for 6 weeks produced no significant improvements in processing speed, episodic memory, or attention in healthy young adults
- Neither moderate (10 g/day) nor high (20 g/day) dosing produced cognitive benefits, ruling out a simple dose-insufficiency explanation for null results in this population
- A trend (p = 0.06) was observed for decreased relative prefrontal cortex (PFC) oxyhemoglobin (O2Hb) in the CR10 group versus placebo during the processing speed test, but this did not reach statistical significance and was not accompanied by behavioral improvement
- Functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) was used to assess PFC activation — no significant changes in PFC O2Hb were observed across groups for any cognitive task
- The null results held across all three cognitive domains tested (processing speed, episodic memory, attention), providing a broadly negative result rather than a domain-specific one

### Methodology
- **Study Type:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, dose-response trial (3 arms)
- **Sample Size:** N=30 (10 per group: CR10, CR20, PLA); M=11, F=19
- **Population/Model:** Healthy young adults (university-aged)
- **Key Methods:** Cognitive test battery (processing speed, episodic memory, attention); functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) to measure prefrontal cortex oxyhemoglobin (O2Hb) during cognitive tasks; pre- and post-supplementation assessments
- **Statistical Methods:** Two-way repeated measures ANOVA (group x time); one-way ANOVA on percentage change scores
- **Duration:** 6 weeks of supplementation

### Evidence Strength Assessment
- **Tier:** Bronze
- **Reasoning:** While the study employs a rigorous design (RCT, double-blind, dose-response, neuroimaging), the very small sample size (N=10 per group) severely limits statistical power to detect moderate effect sizes. The study is likely underpowered for detecting the small cognitive effects (d = 0.09-0.17) that have been suggested by the largest RCT on this topic (Sandkuhler et al., 2023). The inclusion of fNIRS neuroimaging adds mechanistic value but does not compensate for the power limitation. Additionally, two co-authors (Candow and Forbes) have declared conflicts of interest including advisory board memberships with creatine manufacturers (Alzchem and Create), though notably the null result goes against any potential industry bias.
- **Risk of Bias:** Medium — Small sample size creates high risk of Type II error; conflicts of interest exist but cut against the null result; randomization and blinding appear adequate; cognitive battery scope is limited (only 3 domains)

### Specific Claims

1. **Claim:** "Creatine (independent of dosage) had no significant effect on the measures of cognitive performance"
   - **Evidence:** Two-way repeated measures ANOVA across three cognitive domains (processing speed, episodic memory, attention) showed no significant group x time interactions for either creatine dose vs. placebo
   - **Confidence:** Medium (abstract-only; specific p-values and effect sizes not available; small sample limits interpretability of null result)
   - **Section/Page:** Abstract / Results

2. **Claim:** "Six weeks of Cr supplementation at a moderate or high dose does not improve cognitive performance or change PFC activation in young adults"
   - **Evidence:** No significant changes in fNIRS-measured PFC O2Hb across groups during cognitive tasks; only a non-significant trend (p = 0.06) for decreased O2Hb in CR10 vs. PLA during processing speed test
   - **Confidence:** Medium (fNIRS data provides objective neuroimaging corroboration of the behavioral null result, but abstract-only access limits assessment of full statistical details)
   - **Section/Page:** Abstract / Conclusions

3. **Claim:** "A more complex set of cognitive tasks or ones that induce greater mental fatigue are needed to prompt additional brain activity in healthy young adults"
   - **Evidence:** This is the authors' interpretive suggestion, not directly supported by data in this study. It is a post-hoc explanation for the null result, consistent with the broader literature showing creatine benefits mainly under metabolic stress
   - **Confidence:** Low (speculative interpretation, not empirically tested in this study)
   - **Section/Page:** Discussion / Conclusions

4. **Claim:** "Whether an increase in brain creatine levels from creatine supplementation leads to downstream improvements in central nervous system and cognitive functioning in young, healthy adults remains to be fully determined"
   - **Evidence:** The study did not measure brain creatine levels (used fNIRS for hemodynamics, not MRS for metabolites), so this is a broader field-level observation rather than a study-specific finding
   - **Confidence:** Medium (accurately reflects the state of the field)
   - **Section/Page:** Conclusions

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:** The authors acknowledged that the cognitive tasks used may not have been sufficiently demanding to reveal creatine-related benefits in a young, healthy population; they suggested more complex or fatigue-inducing tasks may be needed
- **Additional concerns:**
  - Very small sample size (N=10 per group) creates substantial risk of Type II error; the study is likely underpowered for the small effect sizes reported in the literature (d ~ 0.1-0.2)
  - No measurement of brain creatine levels via MRS, so it is unknown whether supplementation actually increased brain creatine in these participants — a critical gap given that brain creatine uptake is limited by the SLC6A8 transporter
  - The cognitive battery was relatively narrow (3 domains); tasks like Raven's Progressive Matrices or more demanding working memory paradigms have shown effects in other studies
  - No dietary assessment reported (e.g., vegetarian vs. omnivore status), which has been identified as a potential moderator of creatine's cognitive effects
  - No baseline creatine status measurement (serum or dietary)
  - Six-week duration may be insufficient for maximal brain creatine loading given the slow transport kinetics across the blood-brain barrier
  - Conflicts of interest: Two co-authors serve on scientific advisory boards for creatine manufacturers (Alzchem, Create), though the null result direction mitigates this concern

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:** Research questions 1 (Does creatine improve cognitive performance in healthy adults? — answer: no, in this study), 2 (Which cognitive domains are affected? — none in this study), and 3 (What dosing protocols have been studied? — 10 g/day and 20 g/day for 6 weeks both produced null results)
- **Partially addresses:** Research question 4 (proposed mechanisms — the fNIRS data provides indirect evidence that PFC activation is not altered by creatine in resting young adults, though this is hemodynamic rather than metabolic measurement)
- **Novel contribution:** This is one of the few dose-response studies in the creatine-cognition literature, directly testing whether null results in young adults are attributable to insufficient dosing. The inclusion of fNIRS neuroimaging provides an objective biological measure alongside behavioral outcomes. The null result at 20 g/day (a dose shown to increase brain creatine in MRS studies) strengthens the case that healthy young adults may be a ceiling population for creatine's cognitive effects.

Paper_023 had a much larger sample and also found only trivially small effect sizes (d = 0.09-0.17) via Bayesian analysis.
- Agrees with paper_003 (Forbes et al., 2024, Behavioural Brain Research) on: The disconnect between the theoretical mechanism (brain energy augmentation) and empirical cognitive outcomes in healthy populations. Notably, Forbes is an author on both paper_003 and paper_024.
- Agrees with paper_004 (EFSA Panel, 2024) on: Insufficient evidence for cognitive benefits; EFSA specifically noted that cognitive effects were not seen at doses below 20 g/day and were inconsistent even at 20 g/day.
- Contradicts paper_001 (Xu et al., 2024 meta-analysis) on: The meta-analysis found significant positive effects on memory and attention, while this primary RCT found no effects on any domain. However, the meta-analysis included stressed populations and clinical groups, which may explain the discrepancy.
- Contradicts paper_005/paper_025 (Gordji-Nejad et al., 2024) on: That study found creatine improved cognition during sleep deprivation, but the key difference is the stress condition — paper_024 tested resting, non-stressed young adults, supporting the hypothesis that creatine benefits cognition only under metabolic stress.
- Complements paper_002 (Kreider & Stout, 2021) on: The review noted that benefits appear most pronounced under stress conditions; paper_024's null result in non-stressed young adults is consistent with this pattern.
