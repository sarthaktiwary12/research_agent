## Abstract
This systematic review examined the effects of creatine supplementation on cognitive function specifically in healthy individuals. Six randomized controlled trials involving 281 individuals met the inclusion criteria. The review assessed multiple cognitive domains including short-term memory, long-term memory, spatial memory, memory scanning, attention, executive function, response inhibition, word fluency, reaction time, and mental fatigue.

## Key Findings
- **Short-term memory:** Evidence that creatine supplementation may improve short-term memory
- **Intelligence/reasoning:** Evidence that creatine supplementation may improve intelligence/reasoning measures
- **Long-term memory:** Conflicting results
- **Spatial memory:** Conflicting results
- **Memory scanning:** Conflicting results
- **Attention:** Conflicting results
- **Executive function:** Conflicting results
- **Response inhibition:** Conflicting results
- **Word fluency:** Conflicting results
- **Reaction time:** Conflicting results
- **Mental fatigue:** Conflicting results
- **Studies included:** 6 RCTs
- **Participants:** 281 healthy individuals
- **No quantitative meta-analysis was feasible** due to heterogeneity in supplementation protocols and cognitive tasks

## Age and Diet Effects
- **Young adults:** Not affected by creatine supplementation — cognitive performance stayed unchanged
- **Older/stressed individuals:** Potential benefits observed, consistent with the hypothesis that elderly require additional energy for cognitive tasks which creatine may provide
- **Vegetarians:** Responded better than meat-eaters in memory tasks, but no differences for other cognitive domains

## Main Conclusions
There is evidence that creatine supplementation may improve short-term memory and intelligence/reasoning in healthy individuals. However, for most other cognitive domains, results were conflicting and inconclusive. The benefits appear selective for: (1) aging individuals, (2) those under stress (sleep deprivation, hypoxia, mental fatigue), and (3) vegetarians (for memory specifically). Young healthy adults showed no meaningful cognitive benefits.

## Deep Reading Notes

> **Note: Analysis based on abstract only. Evidence confidence is reduced.**

### Key Findings
- Creatine supplementation may improve **short-term memory** in healthy individuals, based on a qualitative synthesis of 6 RCTs (281 participants)
- Creatine supplementation may improve **intelligence/reasoning** measures in healthy individuals
- Results were **conflicting and inconclusive** for the majority of cognitive domains tested: long-term memory, spatial memory, memory scanning, attention, executive function, response inhibition, word fluency, reaction time, and mental fatigue
- **Young healthy adults** showed no meaningful cognitive benefits from creatine supplementation
- **Older adults and individuals under stress** (sleep deprivation, hypoxia, mental fatigue) showed potential cognitive benefits, consistent with a bioenergetic hypothesis that aging and stress increase neural energy demands
- **Vegetarians** responded better than meat-eaters specifically on memory tasks, but not on other cognitive domains — suggesting baseline creatine status may moderate supplementation effects
- **No quantitative meta-analysis was feasible** due to high heterogeneity in supplementation protocols (dose, duration) and cognitive outcome measures across the 6 included trials
- Only 6 RCTs with 281 total participants met inclusion criteria, indicating the evidence base was quite limited at the time of this review (2018)

### Methodology
- **Study Type:** Systematic review of randomized controlled trials (qualitative synthesis only; no meta-analysis performed)
- **Sample Size:** 6 RCTs included, N=281 total participants across all studies
- **Population/Model:** Healthy individuals (adults); subgroups examined by age (young vs. older), diet (vegetarian vs. omnivore), and stress status
- **Key Methods:** Systematic search of databases for RCTs of oral creatine supplementation with cognitive outcomes in healthy populations; narrative/qualitative synthesis of findings across cognitive domains
- **Statistical Methods:** Not fully assessable from abstract — no pooled effect sizes were calculated due to heterogeneity; findings reported as direction of evidence per cognitive domain
- **Duration:** Variable across included studies (not specified in available text)

### Evidence Strength Assessment
- **Tier:** Silver
- **Reasoning:** This is a systematic review (high in evidence hierarchy), but several factors limit it: (1) only 6 RCTs met inclusion criteria, a very small pool; (2) no meta-analysis was possible due to heterogeneity, so conclusions rest on qualitative narrative synthesis; (3) total sample across all studies was only 281 participants; (4) the review was published in a peer-reviewed journal (Experimental Gerontology) and has been widely cited. Under abstract-only reading rules, this would normally qualify as Gold (systematic review of RCTs) but is downgraded to Silver due to the small evidence base, inability to perform meta-analysis, and the abstract-only limitation of this reading.
- **Risk of Bias:** Medium — The systematic review methodology appears sound, but the underlying RCTs were few, small, and heterogeneous. ### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:** Heterogeneity in supplementation protocols and cognitive measures across studies precluded meta-analysis; only 6 RCTs met inclusion criteria (very limited evidence base)
- **Additional concerns:**
  - With only 281 participants across all included studies, the review is underpowered to detect small-to-moderate cognitive effects
  - The inability to perform meta-analysis means no pooled effect sizes, confidence intervals, or formal tests of heterogeneity are available — conclusions rely on subjective narrative synthesis
  - Publication bias cannot be formally assessed (e.g., via funnel plot) without meta-analysis
  - The subgroup conclusions about age, diet, and stress are based on very few studies per subgroup and should be considered hypothesis-generating rather than definitive
  - The review was published in 2018 and therefore does not capture several subsequent RCTs and meta-analyses published 2020-2025
  - Abstract-only reading limits full assessment of search strategy quality, risk of bias assessment of individual included studies, and detailed results per trial

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:**
  - Question 1 (Does creatine supplementation improve cognitive performance in healthy adults?) — This is the central question of the review; the answer is "possibly for short-term memory and reasoning, but not consistently across domains"
  - Question 2 (Which cognitive domains are most affected?) — The review systematically evaluates multiple domains and identifies short-term memory and intelligence/reasoning as the most supported
- **Partially addresses:**
  - Question 3 (What dosing protocols have been studied?) — The review notes heterogeneity in supplementation protocols, but specific dosing details are not available from the abstract
  - Question 4 (What are the proposed neurobiological mechanisms?) — The bioenergetic hypothesis is referenced (elderly need additional energy for cognitive tasks), but mechanistic detail is limited
- **Novel contribution:** This was one of the first systematic reviews to specifically examine cognitive effects of creatine in healthy (non-clinical) populations. It established the important moderating roles of age, stress status, and vegetarian diet — subgroup patterns that have been confirmed and extended by subsequent reviews (e.g., paper_001/paper_020 by Xu et al. 2024, paper_021 by Prokopidis et al. 2023). Its inability to perform meta-analysis highlighted the field's methodological heterogeneity, motivating more standardized protocols in later studies.
