# Effects of creatine on mental fatigue and cerebral hemoglobin oxygenation

## Abstract
Using a double-blind placebo-controlled paradigm, dietary supplementation of creatine (8 g/day for 5 days) was shown to reduce mental fatigue when subjects repeatedly perform a simple mathematical calculation. The study measured cerebral oxygenated hemoglobin using functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) during a unique serial calculation task (Uchida-Kraepelin test). Creatine supplementation resulted in a reduction in mental fatigue and a significant decrease in cerebral oxygenated hemoglobin during the task.

## Key Findings
- **Population:** Healthy young adults; creatine group (n=12) and placebo group (n=12)
- **Design:** Double-blind, placebo-controlled
- **Dose:** 8 g/day creatine for 5 days
- **Cognitive task:** Uchida-Kraepelin test (serial mathematical calculation under time pressure)
- **Primary outcome:** Reduction in mental fatigue with creatine supplementation
- **Neuroimaging:** Significant reduction in cerebral oxygenated hemoglobin measured via fNIRS during the cognitive task in the creatine group
- **Mechanistic interpretation:** The decrease in cerebral oxygenation suggests more efficient utilization of the immediate energy system (phosphocreatine/ATP) and decreased reliance on aerobic metabolism, potentially reflecting increased brain creatine levels

## Significance in the Field
This was one of the earliest studies to demonstrate cognitive effects of creatine supplementation in healthy individuals, and one of the first to use neuroimaging (fNIRS) to provide mechanistic insight. The finding that creatine reduced mental fatigue during a demanding cognitive task has been influential in shaping the "stress/fatigue hypothesis" — that creatine's cognitive benefits are most pronounced under conditions of mental stress or depletion.

## Methodological Concerns
- Very small sample size (N=24 total)
- No direct comparisons between creatine and placebo groups were performed in the statistical analysis (noted as a limitation by subsequent reviews)
- Short supplementation duration (5 days)
- Single cognitive task (Uchida-Kraepelin test) limits generalizability across cognitive domains
- The interpretation of decreased cerebral oxygenation as "more efficient energy use" is speculative and not directly confirmed
