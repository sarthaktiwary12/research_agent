# The influence of creatine supplementation on the cognitive functioning of vegetarians and omnivores

## Abstract
The influence of creatine supplementation on cognitive functioning was examined, contrasting the effect in omnivores and vegetarians. Young adult females (N=128) were separated into those who were and were not vegetarian. Randomly and under a double-blind procedure, subjects consumed either a placebo or 20 g of creatine monohydrate for 5 days (5 g tablets taken throughout the day on an empty stomach). Sixty subjects received placebo and sixty-one received creatine.

## Key Findings
- **Population:** 128 young adult females, separated into vegetarians and omnivores
- **Design:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled (parallel group, not crossover)
- **Dose:** 20 g/day creatine monohydrate for 5 days (loading dose protocol)
- **Cognitive tests:** Memory tasks, verbal fluency, vigilance, choice reaction time
- **Results:**
  - **Memory:** In vegetarians (but not omnivores), creatine supplementation resulted in significantly better memory performance
  - **Verbal fluency and vigilance:** Creatine supplementation did not influence these measures in either group
  - **Reaction time:** Irrespective of dietary style, creatine supplementation decreased the variability in responses to a choice reaction-time task (more consistent responding)
- **Interpretation:** Vegetarians may benefit more from creatine supplementation for memory because they have lower baseline muscle creatine stores (~100 mmol/kg dry muscle vs. ~120 mmol/kg in omnivores), potentially creating a greater relative increase with supplementation

## Significance in the Field
This is the largest single-study examination of the vegetarian/omnivore moderating effect on creatine-cognition outcomes. Its finding that vegetarians selectively benefit on memory tasks has been central to the "baseline creatine depletion" hypothesis — that individuals with lower endogenous creatine stores show greater cognitive response to supplementation. This finding was cited by Avgerinos et al. (2018) as key evidence for the vegetarian subgroup effect. However, the Sandkühler et al. (2023) replication (paper_023) found no significant diet x treatment interaction, calling this finding into question.

## Notable Paradox
Interestingly, unlike skeletal muscle, brain creatine and phosphocreatine levels in vegetarians are not lower compared to omnivores (as shown by MRS studies), and creatine supplementation (0.3 g/kg/day for 7 days) in vegetarians and omnivores failed to increase phosphocreatine levels in the brain. This creates a paradox: if brain creatine levels are similar in vegetarians and omnivores, why would vegetarians show greater cognitive benefit from supplementation? The authors suggest an indirect mechanism, possibly through effects on glucose homeostasis rather than direct brain creatine increases.

## Methodological Concerns
- Parallel-group design (not crossover) reduces within-subject power
- Short supplementation duration (5 days) with high dose (20 g/day) — this is a loading protocol typically used for muscle saturation, not optimized for brain creatine increase
- Female-only sample limits generalizability
- The diet-moderation finding was not replicated in the larger Sandkühler et al. (2023) trial
- The mechanistic paradox (brain creatine levels similar in vegetarians) is unresolved
