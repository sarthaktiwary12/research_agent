## Abstract
This review examines the effects of creatine supplementation on brain function and health, covering the mechanisms by which creatine supports brain bioenergetics and its potential neuroprotective effects. Creatine plays a crucial role in maintaining ATP supply in the brain, especially during times of high demand such as hypoxia, sleep deprivation, mental fatigue, or in psychiatric illnesses that involve brain bioenergetic deficits. The review discusses how creatine enters the cytosol via creatine transporters (CRTs) at the blood-brain barrier, neurons, and oligodendrocytes, contributing to the maintenance of glycolytic ATP levels. Creatine enters the mitochondria via mitochondrial creatine kinases (MtCKs) and converts ATP to phosphocreatine through oxidative phosphorylation. ATP and PCr circulate from the mitochondria back into the cytosol, regulating energy requirements which in turn may enhance brain energy metabolism. Creatine's potential neuroprotective effects are linked to oxidative stress modulation, antioxidant actions, electrophysiological changes, and neurodevelopment. The review covers evidence from clinical trials utilizing MRS showing that changes in brain phosphocreatine corresponded with treatment responses, providing mechanistic evidence that creatine supplementation addresses symptoms by altering brain bioenergetic resources. In individuals with Alzheimer's disease, there is dysfunction in the brain's creatine system with decreased levels of PCr and reduced brain-specific creatine kinase in later stages.
