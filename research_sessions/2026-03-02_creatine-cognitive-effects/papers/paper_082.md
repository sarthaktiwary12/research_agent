## Abstract
This study examined the effect of creatine supplementation and 24-hour sleep deprivation, with mild exercise, on cognitive and psychomotor performance, mood state, and plasma concentrations of catecholamines and cortisol. Subjects were divided into a creatine group (n=10) and a placebo group (n=9). Following 24-hour sleep deprivation, creatine supplementation had a positive effect on mood state and tasks that place a heavy stress on the prefrontal cortex.

## Key Findings
- **Population:** 19 healthy young adults (creatine n=10, placebo n=9)
- **Design:** Double-blind, placebo-controlled
- **Dose:** Not fully specified in available sources (likely loading protocol)
- **Stressor:** 24-hour sleep deprivation with intermittent mild exercise
- **Results:**
  - Creatine supplementation selectively benefited tasks taxing the prefrontal cortex under prolonged wakefulness and mild stress
  - Positive effect on mood state after sleep deprivation
  - Simpler short-term memory tasks (verbal and spatial recall) were unaffected by creatine supplementation
  - Plasma norepinephrine and dopamine increased significantly after 24h sleep deprivation; cortisol decreased significantly — but these hormonal changes did not differ between creatine and placebo groups
- **Interpretation:** Benefits appear to be task-dependent, most pronounced for prefrontal-mediated functions (attention, executive function) rather than simpler recall tasks; the effect is attributed to enhanced energetic support in prefrontal brain regions under conditions of stress-induced energy depletion

## Significance in the Field
This is one of the foundational studies establishing the "stress-condition hypothesis" for creatine and cognition — the idea that creatine supplementation benefits cognitive performance primarily when the brain is under energetic stress (sleep deprivation, hypoxia, mental fatigue). The finding that benefits were selective for prefrontal-cortex-dependent tasks has influenced subsequent study designs and interpretations. This 2006 study, together with McMorris et al. 2007, forms the primary evidence base for the stress-moderation effect noted in multiple reviews (Avgerinos 2018, Kreider & Stout 2021).

## Methodological Concerns
- Very small sample size (N=19)
- No brain creatine measurement to confirm supplementation reached the CNS
- The stress paradigm (sleep deprivation + exercise) introduces multiple confounds
- Specific cognitive tasks and p-values not fully available from abstract
