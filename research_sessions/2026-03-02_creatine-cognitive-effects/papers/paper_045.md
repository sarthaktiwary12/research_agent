## Abstract
This study examined brain total creatine (tCr) levels in primary progressive aphasia (PPA) subtypes using magnetic resonance spectroscopy. The study found that brain total creatine differs between PPA subtypes and correlates with disease severity.

Key references to the creatine-cognition literature:
- Oral creatine supplementation has been shown to improve cognitive function in normal adults (Avgerinos et al., 2018), though results differed by cognitive domain, effect sizes were small to moderate, and no large-scale creatine supplementation studies have been conducted in PPA patients.
- Creatine and phosphocreatine (reported in combination as tCr) are involved in brain energy metabolism and homeostasis (Rae, 2014).
- Creatine is found in the highest concentrations in tissues with high ATP demand, including muscle and brain tissue (Rackayova et al., 2017; Rae, 2014).

Note: This is a clinical population study (PPA patients), not healthy adults. However, it provides relevant context on how brain creatine levels relate to cognitive function and disease, and cites key authors (Avgerinos, Rae) from the creatine-cognition literature.
