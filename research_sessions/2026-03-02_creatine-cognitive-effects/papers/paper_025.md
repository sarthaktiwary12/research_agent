# Single dose creatine improves cognitive performance and induces changes in cerebral high energy phosphates during sleep deprivation

## Abstract
This study examined whether a single high dose of creatine monohydrate could improve cognitive performance during sleep deprivation. Based on the hypothesis that high extracellular creatine availability and increased intracellular energy consumption will temporarily increase central creatine uptake, subjects were orally administered a high single dose of creatine monohydrate (0.35 g/kg body weight) while performing cognitive tests during sleep deprivation. The study also assessed cerebral high energy phosphate levels using 31P-MRS (magnetic resonance spectroscopy).

## Key Findings
- **Short-term memory tasks:** Creatine alleviated sleep deprivation-induced fatigue and improved performance on Working Memory Test (WMT) and SPAN tasks
- **Reaction speed:** Improved performance on Psychomotor Vigilance Task (PVT) after creatine administration
- **Processing time:** Appears to be improved particularly in numeric and language performance 4 hours after creatine administration
- **Brain energy metabolism:** Single dose of creatine induced changes in cerebral high energy phosphates, providing a mechanistic basis for the cognitive effects
- **Timing:** Effects observed approximately 4 hours after administration
- **Dose:** 0.35 g/kg body weight (single dose) — much higher acute dose than typical supplementation

## Study Design
- **Design:** Randomized, placebo-controlled
- **Context:** Sleep deprivation paradigm
- **Dose:** 0.35 g/kg body weight (single acute dose)
- **Cognitive tasks:** Working Memory Test (WMT), SPAN (memory span), Psychomotor Vigilance Task (PVT), numeric and language processing tasks
- **Neuroimaging:** 31P-MRS for cerebral phosphocreatine/ATP measurements
- **Population:** Healthy adults under sleep deprivation

## Main Conclusions
A single high dose of creatine monohydrate can improve cognitive performance during sleep deprivation, particularly in short-term memory and reaction speed domains. The study provides a neurobiological mechanism for these effects by demonstrating changes in cerebral high energy phosphates (measured via 31P-MRS), supporting the hypothesis that creatine's cognitive benefits operate through enhanced brain energy metabolism. This study is particularly important because it shows benefits under metabolic stress (sleep deprivation) and provides mechanistic evidence via neuroimaging.

## Significance
This study uniquely combines behavioral and neuroimaging evidence, showing that creatine's cognitive effects are tied to cerebral bioenergetics. It supports the broader pattern in the literature that creatine supplementation benefits cognition primarily under conditions of metabolic stress, when the brain's energy demands exceed normal supply.
