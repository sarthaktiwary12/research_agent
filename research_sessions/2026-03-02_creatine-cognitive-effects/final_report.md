# Cognitive Effects of Creatine Supplementation in Healthy Adults: What Does the Evidence Actually Show?

## Executive Summary

Creatine monohydrate is one of the most widely studied dietary supplements in the world, with decades of evidence supporting its role in enhancing muscular performance. In recent years, a parallel line of inquiry has investigated whether oral creatine supplementation can also improve cognitive function in healthy adults, motivated by the well-established role of the creatine kinase-phosphocreatine (CK-PCr) system in brain energy metabolism. This review synthesizes evidence from three meta-analyses, two systematic reviews, the largest randomized controlled trial to date, an authoritative regulatory evaluation by the European Food Safety Authority, and multiple foundational primary studies spanning 2002 to 2025.

The central finding of this review is that the evidence for creatine as a cognitive enhancer in healthy adults is substantially weaker than popular accounts suggest. Meta-analyses report a small, statistically significant effect on memory (standardized mean difference of approximately 0.29), but this aggregate signal is driven largely by older adults aged 66-76, who show a large effect (SMD = 0.88), while younger healthy adults show essentially no benefit (SMD = 0.03). The largest and most methodologically rigorous single trial -- a preregistered, double-blind crossover study of 123 participants published in BMC Medicine (Sandkuhler et al., 2023) -- found no significant cognitive improvement in its preregistered frequentist analysis, and its Bayesian analysis estimated any true effect at a trivially small magnitude of 1 to 2.5 IQ-point equivalents. The European Food Safety Authority rejected a health claim for creatine and cognitive improvement in 2024, finding the evidence insufficient to establish a cause-and-effect relationship.

That said, the evidence is not uniformly null. There is a biologically plausible and empirically supported pattern suggesting that creatine supplementation may benefit cognition under conditions of metabolic stress -- sleep deprivation, hypoxia, and mental fatigue -- and in populations with depleted brain energy reserves, particularly older adults. The mechanistic basis for this pattern is sound: the CK-PCr system acts as a rapid temporal and spatial energy buffer in neurons, and oral supplementation can modestly increase brain creatine concentrations by approximately 5-11%. The emerging and paradigm-shifting discovery that creatine may also function as a central neurotransmitter, with evidence of synaptic vesicle storage and stimulation-dependent release, adds a new dimension to the mechanistic picture, though this work remains preclinical and preliminary.

The field is constrained by significant methodological limitations. The average study has only approximately 30 participants, cognitive test batteries are not standardized, dosing protocols vary enormously (from 5 days to 24 weeks, at 2.2 to 20 g/day), and no single well-powered study has directly compared creatine's cognitive effects under stressed versus rested conditions in the same participants. These limitations mean that even the most carefully conducted meta-analyses are pooling heterogeneous data, and the resulting statistical significance should be interpreted with caution. For healthy young adults at rest and under normal dietary conditions, the current evidence does not support meaningful cognitive enhancement from creatine supplementation.

## Key Findings

1. **Creatine supplementation does not produce meaningful cognitive improvement in young, healthy, non-stressed adults.** Multiple meta-analyses and the largest RCT converge on this conclusion with medium-high confidence (Evidence: Silver).

2. **Memory is the cognitive domain most consistently supported by meta-analytic evidence, with a small pooled effect size of SMD = 0.29.** However, this effect is fragile (lower confidence interval bound of 0.04), marked by high heterogeneity (I-squared = 66%), and driven by older adult subgroups (Evidence: Silver).

3. **Older adults aged 66-76 show a large effect on memory (SMD = 0.88), while younger adults aged 11-31 show a null effect (SMD = 0.03).** This age-dependent pattern is the most striking moderator finding in the literature (Evidence: Silver).

4. **The European Food Safety Authority rejected a health claim linking creatine to cognitive improvement in 2024**, finding the evidence insufficient to establish a cause-and-effect relationship for the general population (Evidence: Silver).

5. **Oral creatine supplementation can increase brain creatine content by approximately 5-11%**, as confirmed by MRS studies. However, this increase does not reliably translate to cognitive improvement in healthy adults (Evidence: Gold for brain creatine increase; Silver for the translation gap).

6. **The CK-PCr energy buffering system plays a crucial and well-established role in brain energy metabolism.** This is foundational biochemistry, not a contested claim (Evidence: Gold).

7. **Creatine's cognitive effects appear most pronounced under conditions of metabolic stress** (sleep deprivation, hypoxia, mental fatigue), consistent with the bioenergetic hypothesis that supplementation fills an energy deficit rather than enhancing already-adequate function (Evidence: Silver).

8. **The previously reported vegetarian advantage for creatine-cognition effects was not replicated in the largest and most rigorous test of this hypothesis** (Sandkuhler et al., 2023, N=123 with approximately 50% vegetarian by design) (Evidence: Silver).

9. **Side effects were 4.25 times more frequent with creatine than placebo** in the largest RCT (p = 0.002), a finding that deserves greater attention in the literature (Evidence: Silver).

10. **Preliminary evidence suggests creatine may function as a central neurotransmitter**, with synaptic vesicle storage and stimulation-dependent release demonstrated in mouse models. If confirmed, this would fundamentally alter the mechanistic framework (Evidence: Bronze).

## Detailed Analysis

### The Overall Efficacy Question: A Nuanced Negative

The question of whether creatine supplementation improves cognitive function in healthy adults does not have a simple yes-or-no answer, but the weight of evidence tilts toward "not meaningfully, under normal conditions." Three meta-analyses have attempted to synthesize the available randomized controlled trial data. Xu et al. (2024) pooled 16 RCTs encompassing 492 participants and found statistically significant improvements in memory, attention time, and processing speed, though the GRADE certainty assessment rated only memory as moderate, with all other domains rated as low certainty. Prokopidis et al. (2023) focused specifically on memory outcomes across 8 RCTs and found a pooled standardized mean difference of 0.29 (95% CI: 0.04-0.53, P = 0.02), indicating a small positive effect. Avgerinos et al. (2018), the earliest systematic review in this space, examined 6 RCTs with 281 participants and found evidence supporting short-term memory and intelligence/reasoning improvements, though heterogeneity precluded quantitative meta-analysis.

Against this meta-analytic backdrop, the individual-study evidence tells a more cautious story. The Sandkuhler et al. (2023) trial, published in BMC Medicine, is the largest and most rigorously designed study on creatine and cognition to date. With 123 healthy adults in a preregistered, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover design, it found no significant improvement on either of its two primary cognitive outcomes: Backward Digit Span (p = 0.064, partial eta-squared = 0.029) and Raven's Advanced Progressive Matrices (p = 0.327, partial eta-squared = 0.008). Notably, the distribution of p-values across all cognitive outcomes was consistent with a null effect. A complementary Bayesian analysis suggested that any true effect, if present, is trivially small -- on the order of Cohen's d = 0.09-0.17, corresponding to approximately 1-2.5 IQ points. This analysis also strongly disfavored the large effect size (d approximately 1.0) reported by the seminal Rae et al. (2003) study that launched the field.

Forbes et al. (2024), in a systematic review published in Behavioural Brain Research, concluded directly that "current research fails to robustly support the theoretical basis for creatine's effect on cognition," characterizing the evidence as "somewhat equivocal." The EFSA Panel (2024) reached a concordant conclusion from a regulatory perspective, rejecting the proposed health claim after reviewing the totality of evidence. The Panel specifically noted that working memory effects appeared only at 20 g/day for 5-7 days in two studies, were not observed at lower doses (2.2-14 g/day), and were not maintained with chronic consumption. One study identified by EFSA showed only 1 of 17 cognitive endpoints reaching significance without correction for multiple comparisons -- a result entirely consistent with chance.

### Who Benefits: The Population Paradox

Perhaps the most important insight from the literature is that creatine's cognitive effects are not uniform across populations. There is a clear and consistent pattern: positive findings cluster in older adults and individuals under metabolic stress, while null findings cluster in young, healthy adults at rest.

The age moderation effect is striking. Prokopidis et al. (2023) found that older adults aged 66-76 showed a large effect size for memory improvement (SMD = 0.88, 95% CI: 0.22-1.55, P = 0.009), while younger participants aged 11-31 showed essentially zero effect (SMD = 0.03, 95% CI: -0.14 to 0.20, P = 0.72, I-squared = 0%). The mechanistic explanation is plausible: age-related declines in both endogenous brain creatine synthesis and dietary creatine intake may create a deficit that supplementation can partially restore. However, this subgroup finding is based on very few studies with extremely high heterogeneity (I-squared = 83% in the older adult subgroup), so the precise magnitude of benefit remains uncertain.

The stress-condition hypothesis, initially established by McMorris et al. (2006, 2007), proposes that creatine supplementation primarily benefits cognition when the brain's energy system is stressed beyond its basal capacity. McMorris et al. (2006) found that after 24 hours of sleep deprivation, creatine supplementation selectively improved performance on tasks taxing the prefrontal cortex and improved mood, while simpler memory tasks were unaffected. McMorris et al. (2007) extended this to 36 hours of sleep deprivation, finding improvements in executive function but not simple memory or alertness. Gordji-Nejad et al. (2024) added neuroimaging evidence to this picture, demonstrating both cognitive improvement and changes in cerebral high-energy phosphate levels after a single high-dose creatine administration during sleep deprivation. This convergence across multiple stress paradigms is consistent with the bioenergetic model: the CK-PCr system is most critical when energy demand exceeds basal supply.

The vegetarian hypothesis -- that individuals with lower dietary creatine intake would show greater cognitive benefit from supplementation -- received early support from Benton and Donohoe (2011), who found that vegetarian women (but not omnivores) showed memory improvement after 5 days of 20 g/day creatine. This was cited by Avgerinos et al. (2018) as evidence for a dietary moderation effect. However, the Sandkuhler et al. (2023) trial, which was specifically designed to test this hypothesis with approximately 60 vegetarian participants in a crossover design, found no significant diet-by-treatment interaction. The failure to replicate in a larger, more rigorous trial substantially weakens the vegetarian advantage claim. An unresolved paradox compounds this: MRS studies show that brain creatine levels in vegetarians are not actually lower than in omnivores, unlike muscle creatine, leaving the theoretical basis for a vegetarian advantage unclear.

### Neurobiological Mechanisms: Plausible but Insufficient

The theoretical rationale for creatine as a cognitive enhancer rests primarily on the CK-PCr system, which serves as a spatial and temporal energy buffer in neurons. Phosphocreatine regenerates ATP approximately 12 times faster than oxidative phosphorylation and 70 times faster than de novo pathways (Forbes et al., 2023). This system is critical during transient peaks in neural energy demand, such as during intense cognitive processing. The importance of this system for brain function is unambiguous -- it is established biochemistry supported by Gold-tier evidence.

The question is whether exogenous creatine supplementation can meaningfully augment this system. Beard and Braissant (2010) provided the foundational review of how creatine reaches the brain. The creatine transporter SLC6A8 is expressed on blood-brain barrier endothelial cells but is absent from the astrocytic feet that cover approximately 99% of the BBB surface, severely restricting peripheral creatine entry into the CNS. Consequently, the brain relies substantially on its own endogenous creatine synthesis via AGAT and GAMT enzymes, primarily in glial cells. Oral supplementation can increase brain creatine content by a modest 5-11% (compared to 20-40% in muscle), and this increase is slow, requiring at least one week and often several weeks of sustained supplementation.

Smith et al. (2025) confirmed this in the first Alzheimer's disease study, finding an average 11% brain creatine increase after 8 weeks at 20 g/day, with 85% of participants showing measurable increases. Forbes et al. (2024) acknowledged this brain loading capacity but emphasized the central disconnect: "increased brain creatine does not automatically translate to improved cognitive function." The Sandkuhler et al. (2023) null result in young healthy adults at a dose (5 g/day for 6 weeks) known to be sufficient for brain creatine increase underscores this gap between biological mechanism and behavioral outcome.

A potentially paradigm-shifting development is the emerging evidence that creatine may function as a central neurotransmitter. Research published in bioRxiv (2022) and subsequently in eLife (2023) detected creatine in synaptic vesicles at concentrations between glutamate/GABA and acetylcholine/serotonin, demonstrated calcium-dependent release upon neuronal depolarization, and found that creatine inhibited approximately one-third of cortical pyramidal neurons at 100 micromolar concentration. SLC6A8 and AGAT knockout mice showed significantly reduced vesicular creatine and stimulation-dependent release. These findings satisfy several classical neurotransmitter criteria: vesicular storage, stimulation-dependent release, postsynaptic action, and reuptake mechanism. If confirmed in humans, this would fundamentally expand the mechanistic framework from a simple energy-buffering model to one that includes direct neurotransmitter signaling. However, all evidence is from mouse models, the postsynaptic receptor remains unidentified, and the physiological relevance of 100 micromolar creatine in the synaptic cleft from oral supplementation is uncertain.

### Dosing: No Clear Optimal Protocol

The literature has not converged on an optimal dosing protocol for cognitive outcomes. Studies have used doses ranging from a single acute dose (0.35 g/kg) to 20 g/day for 8 weeks. The EFSA Panel (2024) made an important observation: working memory effects appeared only at the highest dose tested (20 g/day for 5-7 days) and were absent at lower doses (2.2-14 g/day) and with chronic maintenance protocols. Forbes et al. (2023) tested both 10 and 20 g/day for 6 weeks in healthy young adults (N=30) and found no cognitive benefit at either dose, though the study was severely underpowered with only 10 participants per group.

Meta-regression analyses have found no significant dose-response relationship (Prokopidis et al., 2023) and no difference between short-term and long-term supplementation durations (Xu et al., 2024). These null findings likely reflect underpowered subgroup analyses rather than a true absence of dose-response. The slow BBB transport kinetics described by Beard and Braissant (2010) argue against rapid saturation and suggest that extended supplementation should, in principle, produce greater brain creatine accumulation. One intriguing observation is that guanidinoacetic acid (GAA), a creatine precursor, produced a 16.2% brain creatine increase in healthy men at 3 g/day for 28 days (referenced in Smith et al., 2025), potentially offering a more BBB-permeable alternative to creatine monohydrate.

## Contradictions and Debates

The creatine-cognition literature contains several significant contradictions that reflect genuine scientific uncertainty.

The most consequential disagreement is between the meta-analyses, which find statistically significant memory effects, and the largest individual RCT, which finds a null result. Xu et al. (2024) and Prokopidis et al. (2023) aggregate data across 16 and 8 RCTs respectively and detect pooled statistical significance for memory. Sandkuhler et al. (2023), despite being the largest and most carefully designed single study, found no significant improvement in the preregistered frequentist analysis. The Bayesian reconciliation is instructive: a real but trivially small effect (d = 0.09-0.17) may exist that is large enough to reach significance when pooled across many studies but too small for detection in any single study. The practical significance of such an effect -- perhaps 1-2 IQ points -- is questionable.

A revealing internal contradiction exists within the work of a single author group. Forbes et al. published a narrative review in Nutrients in 2023 ("Heads Up") that presented a cautiously positive view of creatine's cognitive potential, stating that supplementation "may help explain some of the positive effects on measures of cognition and memory, especially in aging adults or during times of metabolic stress." One year later, substantially the same author group published a systematic review in Behavioural Brain Research concluding that "current research fails to robustly support the theoretical basis for creatine's effect on cognition." This shift likely reflects genuine evolution in the authors' assessment as larger null results (particularly Sandkuhler et al., 2023) accumulated, and it mirrors the field's broader trajectory from early optimism to greater caution.

The EFSA regulatory rejection and positive meta-analytic findings are not truly contradictory but reflect different evidentiary frameworks. EFSA applies a stringent regulatory standard designed for general population health claims, with a rejection rate exceeding 80% for all submitted claims. The meta-analyses apply standard academic thresholds for statistical significance. Both are legitimate for their respective purposes, but practitioners should understand that "statistically significant in a meta-analysis" does not equate to "sufficient evidence for a health claim."

An intriguing mechanistic tension arises from the neurotransmitter hypothesis. If creatine inhibits cortical pyramidal neurons, as demonstrated in mouse models, how does this reconcile with cognitive enhancement? Inhibition of excitatory neurons is not necessarily detrimental -- it could improve signal-to-noise ratio or modulate excitatory-inhibitory balance -- but the finding adds complexity to a field that has operated primarily on the simpler energy-buffering hypothesis. This tension remains unresolved and represents a frontier for future investigation.

## Evidence Quality Assessment

### Overall Evidence Landscape

The evidence base for creatine's cognitive effects in healthy adults is moderate in quantity but limited in quality. The field has progressed from being too small for meta-analysis in 2018 (Avgerinos et al.) to supporting three quantitative meta-analyses by 2024, but the underlying primary studies remain small, heterogeneous, and methodologically inconsistent. This is a field where the meta-analytic signal outpaces the reliability of the individual data points.

### Strengths of the Evidence Base

The mechanistic framework is well-grounded in established biochemistry. The CK-PCr system's role in brain energy metabolism is Gold-tier evidence, and the ability of oral creatine to increase brain creatine content is confirmed across multiple MRS studies. The field has produced one genuinely well-designed large RCT (Sandkuhler et al., 2023) with both frequentist and Bayesian analyses, an authoritative regulatory evaluation (EFSA, 2024), and multiple independent meta-analyses. The convergence of null findings in young healthy adults across different research groups and study designs provides robust evidence for this specific negative conclusion.

### Weaknesses of the Evidence Base

The average study in the meta-analyses contains only approximately 31 participants, far below the sample sizes needed to detect small cognitive effects reliably. Cognitive outcome measures are not standardized -- studies use different test batteries, different outcome definitions, and different statistical approaches. Dosing protocols vary enormously, making dose-response analysis unreliable. High heterogeneity in the meta-analyses (I-squared = 66-83%) signals that the pooled estimates may not reflect a single underlying effect. A published critique raised concerns about "double-counting due to inadequate statistics" in the Prokopidis et al. (2023) meta-analysis, which could produce false-positive findings. All evidence in this review was limited to abstract-level access due to systematic full-text retrieval failures (HTTP 403), reducing confidence in detailed methodological assessments.

## Knowledge Gaps and Uncertainties

- **Within-study stress comparison:** No single well-powered study has directly compared creatine's cognitive effects under stressed versus rested conditions in the same participants. The stress-moderation hypothesis is supported only by between-study comparisons, which are confounded by many other design differences.

- **Safety data depth:** Only 3-4 sources address adverse effects at cognitive-relevant doses (10-20 g/day). The Sandkuhler et al. finding that side effects are 4.25 times more frequent with creatine than placebo is notable and underexplored. No systematic review of adverse effects specifically at these higher doses exists.

- **Optimal brain-loading protocol:** The relationship between MRS-measured brain creatine increase and cognitive outcome magnitude has not been systematically characterized. Whether loading protocols, maintenance doses, or alternative delivery agents (such as GAA) could change the cognitive outcome picture remains unclear.

- **Neurotransmitter hypothesis validation in humans:** The creatine-as-neurotransmitter evidence comes from a single research group using mouse models. The postsynaptic receptor is unidentified, and no studies have examined whether oral supplementation affects this neurotransmitter function in human brains.

- **Long-term cognitive effects:** Most studies span 5 days to 6 weeks. The effects of sustained creatine supplementation over months or years on cognitive trajectories, particularly in aging populations where the largest benefits are suggested, remain essentially unstudied.

- **Publication bias:** No formal publication bias assessment (funnel plots, Egger's test) was accessible from the meta-analyses. The field may be susceptible to positive-result publication bias given supplement industry interest.

## Recommendations

### For Practitioners and Decision-Makers

The current evidence does not support recommending creatine supplementation as a cognitive enhancer for healthy young adults under normal conditions. The expected cognitive benefit, if any, is on the order of 1-2 IQ points -- a magnitude of questionable practical significance. For individuals who are elderly (over 65), regularly sleep-deprived, or under sustained cognitive stress, there is suggestive but not definitive evidence that creatine may provide modest cognitive benefits, particularly for memory tasks. Any supplementation decision should weigh these uncertain benefits against the 4.25-fold increase in side effects reported in the largest trial, the inconvenience of the high doses likely needed (20 g/day), and the slow onset of brain creatine loading (at least 1-2 weeks).

### For Researchers

The field's greatest need is for large, preregistered RCTs (N > 200) that use standardized cognitive batteries and include both stressed and non-stressed conditions in the same within-subjects design. Concurrent MRS measurement of brain creatine would allow direct testing of whether individuals with greater brain creatine increases show greater cognitive improvement. Studies in older adults (65+) are particularly warranted given the meta-analytic signal for this population. The neurotransmitter hypothesis demands human neuroimaging and electrophysiological investigation. Finally, systematic dose-response studies comparing creatine monohydrate to alternative delivery agents like GAA could determine whether the modest BBB permeability of creatine is a remediable bottleneck.

### For Further Reading

Readers seeking to enter this literature should begin with the following five papers, which collectively capture the full range of perspectives:

1. **Sandkuhler et al. (2023), BMC Medicine** -- The largest and most rigorous RCT, providing the strongest null-leaning evidence with Bayesian nuance.
2. **Prokopidis et al. (2023), Nutrition Reviews** -- The most focused meta-analysis on memory, revealing the critical age moderation effect.
3. **Forbes et al. (2024), Behavioural Brain Research** -- The most skeptical systematic review, articulating the theory-evidence disconnect.
4. **EFSA Panel (2024), EFSA Journal** -- The authoritative regulatory assessment rejecting the cognitive health claim.
5. **Beard and Braissant (2010), Journal of Neurochemistry** -- The foundational review of how creatine reaches the brain, essential for understanding the biological constraints.
