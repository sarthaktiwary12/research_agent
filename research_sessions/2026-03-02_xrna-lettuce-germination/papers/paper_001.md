## Abstract
tRNA-derived small RNAs (tsRNAs), first considered as byproducts of tRNA random degradation, have been characterized in all three kingdoms of life as a new class of regulatory small non-coding RNAs involved in a wide range of biological processes. This review covers the biogenesis, classification, and functions of tRFs in plants with a focus on biotic and abiotic stress responses. tRFs are classified into 5'tRF (17-26 nt from D-loop cleavage), 3'tRF (from T-loop cleavage), 3'U-tRF (from precursor 3' trailer), and tRNA-halves (30-40 nt from anticodon loop cleavage). In plants, >99.8% of tRFs originate from mature tRNAs rather than pre-tRNAs. Key biogenesis enzymes include the RNS family and DCL1 (for male gamete-specific tRFs). The abundance of tRFs is dynamically modulated by various stresses including oxidation, heat, drought, high salinity, UV stress, phosphate deficiency, and pathogen attacks. tRFs associate with AGO proteins (particularly AGO1) to mediate post-transcriptional silencing of target mRNAs and transposable elements.

### Key Findings
- tRNA-derived small RNAs (tsRNAs) are a distinct class of regulatory non-coding RNAs, not mere degradation products, found across all three kingdoms of life
- In plants, >99.8% of tRFs originate from mature tRNAs rather than pre-tRNAs, indicating specific processing rather than random degradation
- tRFs are classified into four main types based on cleavage site: 5'tRF (17-26 nt, from D-loop cleavage), 3'tRF (from T-loop cleavage), 3'U-tRF (from precursor 3' trailer via RNase Z), and tRNA-halves (30-40 nt, from anticodon loop cleavage)
- Biogenesis enzymes include the RNS family (Dicer-independent, ABA-induced) and DCL1 (for male gamete-specific tRFs in pollen)
- tRF abundance is dynamically modulated by oxidative stress, heat, drought, high salinity, UV stress, phosphate deficiency, and pathogen attacks
- 5' tRNA halves from tRNA-His(GTG), tRNA-Arg(CCT), and tRNA-Trp(GTA) are specifically induced under oxidative stress in Arabidopsis (shown by Northern blot)
- tRFs associate with AGO proteins, particularly AGO1, to mediate post-transcriptional silencing of target mRNAs and transposable elements
- 19-nt tRF-5s accumulate specifically in pollen, processed by DCL1, and loaded into AGO1 to target Gypsy LTR retrotransposon transcripts
- Cross-kingdom gene silencing: rhizobial tRFs transfer to soybean roots and hijack host RNAi machinery to silence host genes, enhancing nodulation

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:** Research into tsRNAs faces challenges in accurately and efficiently interfering with or quantifying their expression to interpret their exact functions and mechanisms
- **Additional concerns:** (1) The review is abstract-only accessible, so deeper methodological critique is not possible. (2) The review does not specifically address G-quadruplex formation in tRFs or its role in plant gene silencing. (3) Most AGO1-tRF silencing evidence comes from Arabidopsis pollen — generalizability to other tissues and plant species (especially lettuce) is uncertain. (4) The review does not distinguish clearly between mRNA cleavage vs. translational repression as silencing mechanisms.

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:** The mechanism of tRF-mediated gene silencing through AGO1 loading, which is central to understanding how the xRNA drug (G-rich tRFs) could silence target mRNAs in lettuce. The classification of tRF types (17-26 nt 5'tRFs) aligns with the xRNA drug specification (16-22 nt).
- **Partially addresses:** Cross-kingdom RNA transfer (rhizobial tRFs to soybean) provides precedent for exogenous tRFs entering plant cells and utilizing endogenous silencing machinery — analogous to xRNA drug uptake. Stress-responsive tRF modulation relates to the defense-to-growth reallocation hypothesis.
- **Novel contribution:** Provides the foundational framework for understanding tRF biogenesis pathways (DCL1-dependent vs. RNS1-dependent) which may determine how exogenous xRNA tRFs are processed after endosomal escape in lettuce cells.
