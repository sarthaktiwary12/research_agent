## Abstract
Plants have evolved an array of defenses against pathogens, but mounting a defense response frequently comes with the cost of a reduction in growth and reproduction. Most well-characterized growth-defense trade-offs stem from antagonistic crosstalk among hormones rather than identified metabolic expenditure. A primary way plants mitigate costs is through restricted expression of resistance — inducible expression, priming, or transgenerational induction. Fitness trials show that encoding a resistance gene in the absence of infection reduced seed set by 5-10%. The high fitness costs associated with single R genes suggest overstimulation or misregulation of the immune system. Interactions between R genes can coordinate immune responses but misregulation leads to autoimmunity — deleterious hybrid necrosis found in 2% of Arabidopsis crosses.
