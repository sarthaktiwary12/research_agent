## Abstract
The lateral emergence of distinct classes of non-coding RNAs has led to better insights into the eukaryotic regulatory networks. The existence of transfer RNA (tRNA)-derived non-coding RNAs (tncRNAs) demands their exploration in the plant kingdom. The authors designed a methodology to uncover the bigger picture of tncRNAome in plants. They identified diverse tncRNAs of length 14-50 nt in approximately 2,500 small RNA sequencing samples across six major angiosperms. Codon-dependent generation of tncRNAs indicates that the process is highly specific rather than being random tRNA degradation. Analysis of nucleotide composition at cleavage positions indicates they are generated through precise endoribonucleolytic machinery.

## Key Findings
- Identified diverse tRNA-derived ncRNAs (tncRNAs) of 14-50 nt length across six major angiosperm species
- tncRNA generation is codon-dependent, indicating highly specific biogenesis rather than random tRNA degradation
- Precise endoribonucleolytic cleavage machinery generates tncRNAs at specific positions
- ~2,500 small RNA-seq samples analyzed across plant species
- Reveals hidden regulatory circuits involving tRNA-derived fragments in plant gene regulation

## Relevance to xRNA Research
Directly relevant to understanding tRNA fragment (tRF) biology in plants. The finding that tncRNA generation is specific and codon-dependent supports the biological plausibility of G-rich tRF-based xRNA drugs acting through defined gene silencing mechanisms in plant cells.
