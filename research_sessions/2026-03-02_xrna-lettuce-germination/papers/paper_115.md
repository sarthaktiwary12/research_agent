## Abstract
Angiogenin is a stress-activated ribonuclease that cleaves tRNA to produce bioactive small noncoding RNAs (tiRNAs) that function in a cytoprotective stress response program. Point mutations reducing its ribonuclease activity are found in ALS patients. Selected tiRNAs assume G-quadruplex (G4) structures necessary for cytoprotective and prosurvival functions. Stable DNA analogs of these G4-containing tiRNAs spontaneously enter motor neurons and confer cytoprotection. Selected 5'-tiRNAs (tiRNA-Ala, tiRNA-Cys) cooperate with translational repressor YB-1 to displace eIF4F from capped mRNA, inhibit translation initiation, and induce stress granule assembly. Translationally active tiRNAs assemble unique G-quadruplex structures required for translation inhibition. tiRNA-Ala binds the cold shock domain of YB-1.

## Key Relevance to xRNA Project
- DEFINES the G4-tRF mechanism in mammalian systems: G-quadruplex formation is REQUIRED for tRF-mediated translational repression
- The 5'-TOG motif (terminal oligoguanine) drives G4 formation — directly relevant to xRNA drug G-rich design
- YB-1 is the mammalian cofactor — the key question is whether a plant YB-1 orthologue exists (plant CSP/CSD proteins?)
- eIF4F displacement mechanism: G4-tRFs compete for cap-binding complex — in plants this would be eIF(iso)4F
- G4-DNA analogs can SPONTANEOUSLY enter cells — relevant to xRNA drug uptake, though the xRNA drug uses nucleolin-mediated endocytosis
- Key CAVEAT from Jackowiak 2017: this mechanism does NOT work the same way in plant systems (wheat germ extract)
