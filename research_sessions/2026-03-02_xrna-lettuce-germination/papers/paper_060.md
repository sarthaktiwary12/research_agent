## Abstract
tRNA-derived fragments (tRFs) are an emerging class of conserved small non-coding RNAs that play important roles in post-transcriptional gene regulation. Significant advances in high-throughput sequencing technologies have enabled the discovery of new classes of small RNAs in plants, and among them, tRNA-derived fragments (tRFs) arise as new enigmatic molecular players. tsRNAs can be divided into three major types depending on length, cleavage site, and precursor type: 5' or 3' tRHs (tRNA-derived halves) derived from cleavage of mature tRNAs at the anticodon loop (30-35 nt); 5', 3', or inter tRFs derived from cleavage at the D and/or TpsiC loop (10-30 nt); and 3'U tRF derived from pre-tRNA cleavage by RNase Z. tRFs function through multiple mechanisms including translation inhibition (via polyribosome association), AGO protein interactions for transcriptional and post-transcriptional gene silencing, interaction with splicing factor SR34, transposable element regulation, and cross-kingdom gene silencing. In Arabidopsis, 5' tRF-Ala-AGC inhibits protein synthesis in a cap-independent manner requiring G18 and G19 residues. Different stress conditions induce tissue-specific tRF production, e.g., phosphate stress induces expression in roots while oxidative stress induces expression in flowers.
