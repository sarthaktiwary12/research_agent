## Abstract
Guanine-rich sequences are able to form complex RNA structures termed RNA G-quadruplexes in vitro, and because of their high stability, they are proposed to exist in vivo and are associated with important biological relevance. The study profiled the landscape of guanine-rich regions with in vitro folding potential in the Arabidopsis transcriptome. They found a global enrichment of RNA G-quadruplexes with two G-quartets, whereby folding potential is strongly influenced by RNA secondary structures. Using in vitro and in vivo RNA chemical structure profiling, they determined that hundreds of RNA G-quadruplex structures are strongly folded in both Arabidopsis and rice, providing direct evidence of RNA G-quadruplex formation in living plant cells.

## Key Findings
- Direct evidence of RNA G-quadruplex (rG4) formation in vivo in Arabidopsis and rice
- Global enrichment of rG4s with two G-quartets across the plant transcriptome
- rG4 folding potential is influenced by local RNA secondary structure context
- Hundreds of strongly folded rG4 structures detected using chemical profiling
- In vitro and in vivo profiling methods validated for plant rG4 detection

## Relevance to xRNA Research
Foundational for understanding the xRNA drug's G-quadruplex structure. The xRNA drug is described as a G-rich tRF forming stable parallel G-quadruplexes. This paper provides direct evidence that RNA G-quadruplexes exist and function in vivo in plant cells, validating the structural basis for xRNA drug stability and potential functional interactions within plant cellular environments. The finding that two-quartet rG4s are globally enriched suggests that the xRNA drug's G4 structure could engage with endogenous rG4-binding proteins or pathways.

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:** Not available from abstract
- **Additional concerns:** (1) Preprint from 2019 — status of journal publication unclear; results may have been refined; (2) "Hundreds" of rG4s out of the entire transcriptome is a relatively small fraction — most G-rich sequences may not fold into stable rG4 in vivo, potentially due to helicase activity (cf. Guo & Bartel 2016); (3) Two-quartet rG4 enrichment suggests plant rG4s are less stable than the three-quartet structures typically formed by the xRNA drug's G-rich tRFs; (4) Focus on endogenous transcripts — does not address whether exogenous G4-forming small RNAs would be recognized or processed differently; (5) No analysis of rG4 in tRNA-derived fragments specifically; (6) "Function" is claimed in the title but abstract primarily demonstrates existence; functional consequences remain less clear

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:** Whether RNA G-quadruplex structures exist in vivo in plant cells — yes, providing a cellular environment where the xRNA drug's G4 structure would be recognized by endogenous G4-binding proteins and pathways
- **Partially addresses:** Whether G4 formation affects gene regulation in plants (implied by "function" in title but not detailed in abstract); whether plant cells have the machinery to interact with G4 RNA structures
- **Novel contribution:** Establishes that plant cells maintain hundreds of rG4 structures in a folded state in vivo, meaning the cellular environment does not globally unfold all rG4s (contra Guo & Bartel 2016 in mammalian cells). This supports the hypothesis that the xRNA drug's G4 structure would persist inside plant cells, maintaining both its stability and potential silencing-marking function.
