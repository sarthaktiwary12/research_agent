## Abstract
Nucleolin is the major nucleolar protein of growing eukaryotic cells, a multifunctional phosphoprotein acting through interactions with proteins and nucleic acids. It consists of three structural domains: an N-terminal portion with acidic stretches; two to four RNA-binding domains (RRMs) in the central region; and a glycine/arginine-rich (GAR) domain at the C-terminus. Animal nucleolin possesses four RRMs, whereas yeast and plant homologs possess two. Plant nucleolins have a longer GAR domain than mammalian nucleolins. Nucleolin contains intrinsic DNA and RNA helicase, nucleic-acid-dependent ATPase, and self-cleaving activities. In plants, nucleolin genes are found in all species examined (rice, sorghum, maize, poplar, Arabidopsis). Arabidopsis nucleolin (NUC1/NUC2) affects plant development and patterning, and participates in a snoRNP complex competent for rRNA gene binding and pre-rRNA processing. On the cell surface (studied primarily in animal systems), nucleolin acts as a receptor for extracellular nucleic acids (CpG ODNs, poly(I:C), mitochondrial DNA), viruses, bacteria, and toxins. The extracellular nucleic acid-binding role of nucleolin in plants remains largely unexplored.
