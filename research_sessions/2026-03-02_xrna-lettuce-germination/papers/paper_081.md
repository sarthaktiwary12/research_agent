## Abstract
Transfer RNA-derived fragments (tRFs) are an emerging class of small non-coding RNAs derived from precursor or mature tRNAs by specific cleavage. In plants, the abundance of tRF molecules varies among tissues, developmental stages, and environmental conditions. These tRNA-derived fragments are specific small non-coding RNAs with distinct sequence structure, specific expression patterns, and biological roles independent of their parental tRNAs. This review covers recent advances in understanding plant tRFs, focusing on their biogenesis, classification, biological and cellular functions such as regulating stress responses, mediating plant-pathogen interactions, and modulating post-transcriptional gene silencing and translation. Cross-kingdom transcription modulation was revealed between Rhizobia and soybean, where tRFs produced by bacteria are capable of silencing specific host genes. In soybean, rhizobial tRFs found in symbiotic roots are loaded into AGO1, targeting transcripts responsible for root development.
