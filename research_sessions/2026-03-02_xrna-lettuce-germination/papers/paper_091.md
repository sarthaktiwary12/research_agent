## Abstract
Only a fraction of trade-offs that are understood mechanistically to date involve nutrient limitation. A major insight from molecular biological studies is that growth and immunity are tightly coregulated, and many negative correlations between growth and defense are the result of regulatory crosstalk. Plants use several strategies to minimize costs: controlling the timing and duration in which the immune response is active, enabling a plant to focus the immune response to times when it is most needed. Plants can limit the costs of resistance by expressing some defenses only upon infection; indeed, two central modes of plant immunity are induced rather than constitutive, including MAMP/PAMP-triggered immunity. Defense priming can promote rather than inhibit plant development, and in certain cases can drive developmental processes and promote plant yield. The review provides a framework for understanding how plants can mitigate growth-defense tradeoffs through temporal, spatial, and intensity-based regulation of immune responses.
