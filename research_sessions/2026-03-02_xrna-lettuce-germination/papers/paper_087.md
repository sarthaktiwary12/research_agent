## Abstract
G-quadruplexes (G4s) are extremely stable DNA or RNA secondary structures formed by sequences rich in guanine. These structures are implicated in many essential cellular processes, with the number of biological functions attributed to them continuing to grow. RNA G-quadruplexes (rG4s) are more compact and thermally stable than DNA G4s because of the presence of a 2'-hydroxyl group. rG4s can only adopt a parallel conformation due to anti-conformation of glycosidic linkages in ribonucleosides. G4 structures have been found to modulate the Argonaute/RISC pathway at multiple levels: G4s near Drosha cleavage sites regulate pri-miRNA processing; G4s in pre-miRNAs compete with the canonical hairpin to block Dicer recognition and cleavage; both RNA and DNA G-quadruplexes bind human Dicer in vitro, efficiently inhibiting pre-miRNA cleavage; miRNA rG4s can abolish loading onto RISC; and G4 structures in mRNA 3'-UTRs can block miRNA-RISC access to target sites. The review discusses rG4 roles across ncRNA classes including piRNAs, miRNAs, and lncRNAs.
