## Abstract
This landmark 2024 study published in Science examined the molecular defense-growth tradeoff across plant species at the genomic level. The study found marked variation across plant species in the number of nucleotide-binding leucine-rich repeat (NLR) receptors, which are central to plant immune systems. The key finding is that the more a plant species invests in defense (NLR gene repertoires), the less potential it has for growth. This demonstrates significant allocation costs in the generation and maintenance of biodiversity. Importantly, the study also examined cultivated plants bred for specific traits and found that in crop plants, the negative correlation between growth and defense was NOT observed — due to breeding that reduced natural variation in genomes. This finding has direct implications for understanding how defense gene suppression (e.g., through tRF-mediated silencing) could enhance growth traits and seed vigor. The study illuminates mechanisms limiting the evolution of defense.
