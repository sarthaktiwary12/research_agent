## Abstract
According to current textbooks, the principal task of tRNAs and rRNAs is synthesizing proteins. During the last decade, additional cellular roles for precisely processed tRNA and rRNA fragments have become evident in all kingdoms of life. These RNA fragments were originally overlooked in transcriptome datasets or regarded as unspecific degradation products. Upon closer inspection, they were found to engage in a variety of cellular processes, in particular the modulation of translation and the regulation of gene expression by sequence complementarity- and Argonaute protein-dependent gene silencing. The review discusses the role of tRNA- and rRNA-derived fragments in plant-microbe interactions, noting that functional studies on plant tsRNAs are relatively limited compared to animal systems, but a few results show that tsRNAs in plants share similar modes of action, such as RNA silencing and translation inhibition. The lack of mechanistic and functional data for most tRFs leaves open the question of whether tRFs evolved in parallel with miRNAs as an alternative source of RNA guides for gene regulation. This review also highlights the challenges posed by tRNA chemical modifications for sequencing-based detection.
