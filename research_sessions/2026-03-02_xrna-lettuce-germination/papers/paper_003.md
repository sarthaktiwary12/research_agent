## Abstract
This review focuses on the role of tRNA-derived fragments (tRFs) and ribosomal RNA-derived fragments (rRFs) in plant-microbe interactions. Originally considered as artifacts or unspecific degradation products, precisely processed tRNA and rRNA fragments have been found in all kingdoms of life and assigned specific functions. In the context of plant-microbe interactions, rhizobial tRFs can transfer to soybean roots and hijack the host RNA silencing machinery to down-regulate plant RNAs. Bacteria deliver tRFs to plant root cells to modulate host gene expression. The review also discusses the broader significance of these small RNA fragments in symbiotic and pathogenic interactions between plants and microorganisms, highlighting their role as cross-kingdom gene regulators. This represents a paradigm shift in understanding small RNA biology beyond canonical miRNAs and siRNAs.

### Key Findings
- tRNA and rRNA fragments were originally overlooked in transcriptome datasets or regarded as unspecific degradation products, but upon closer inspection have been found to engage in specific cellular functions across all kingdoms of life
- Precisely processed tRNA and rRNA fragments participate in two key processes: (1) modulation of translation and (2) Argonaute protein-dependent gene silencing
- In plant-microbe interactions, rhizobial tRFs transfer to soybean roots and hijack host RNA silencing machinery to downregulate plant RNAs — a paradigm for cross-kingdom tRF-mediated gene regulation
- Bacteria deliver tRFs to plant root cells to modulate host gene expression, representing a novel class of cross-kingdom signal molecules
- tRNA and rRNA fragments may be transferred between organisms via extracellular vesicles (EVs), suggesting a delivery route for cross-kingdom small RNA exchange
- The discovery of tRF and rRF roles in plant-microbe interactions represents a paradigm shift beyond canonical miRNAs and siRNAs in understanding small RNA biology
- The companion study (Kusch et al. 2023) found enrichment of specific 31-33 base 5'-terminal fragments of barley 5.8S ribosomal RNA in extracellular vesicles during barley-powdery mildew interaction, suggesting EVs as a delivery vehicle

### Limitations
- **Stated by authors:** Not assessable from abstract alone
- **Additional concerns:** (1) Abstract-only access severely limits evaluation of the review's depth and specific mechanistic discussions. (2) The review appears to focus on plant-microbe interactions, which may limit coverage of endogenous tRF functions in non-stress contexts (e.g., germination). (3) The EV-mediated delivery hypothesis is still emerging and lacks the level of mechanistic detail needed to inform xRNA drug design. (4) The review does not appear to address G-quadruplex structures in tRFs, which is a key feature of the xRNA drug. (5) The review likely does not cover nucleolin-mediated uptake, which is the presumed xRNA drug entry mechanism, as this is a mammalian/recently-discovered plant pathway. (6) The connection between plant-microbe tRF transfer and exogenous therapeutic RNA delivery is indirect.

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:** Cross-kingdom RNA transfer as a biological phenomenon — the xRNA drug is essentially a synthetic cross-kingdom RNA intervention. The review's coverage of tRF-mediated gene silencing via AGO proteins in plants is directly relevant to the xRNA mechanism of action.
- **Partially addresses:** The EV-mediated delivery route discussed in the review is distinct from but conceptually parallel to the glyco-protected nucleolin-mediated uptake of the xRNA drug. Both involve extracellular RNA crossing cell membranes. The review's discussion of plant pathogen defense responses relates to the "defense-to-growth resource reallocation" question in the brief.
- **Novel contribution:** This paper's unique contribution is framing tRF transfer in the context of plant-microbe interactions, which provides biological precedent for the concept that exogenous small RNAs (including tRFs) can enter plant cells and hijack endogenous silencing machinery. The EV delivery angle may be relevant to understanding how the xRNA drug traffics after endosomal escape.
