# Identification and Characterization of (E)-beta-Caryophyllene Synthase and alpha/beta-Pinene Synthase Potentially Involved in Constitutive and Herbivore-Induced Terpene Formation in Cotton

- **Year:** 2013

## Abstract
Cotton (Gossypium hirsutum L.) plants damaged by insects emit a blend of volatiles, including monoterpenes and sesquiterpenes, which can directly repel herbivores and/or indirectly protect the plant by attracting natural enemies of the herbivores. Recombinant GhTPS1 accepts farnesyl pyrophosphate as substrate and produces (E)-beta-caryophyllene and alpha-humulene. GhTPS1 and GhTPS2 gene expression was elevated after methyl jasmonate (MeJA) treatment in cotton leaves. Beta-caryophyllene is induced by herbivory and fungal elicitors across multiple species, serving as a key volatile in both direct and indirect plant defense.

### Metabolic Cost Analysis (Key for Research Brief)
- **FPP flux competition:** FPP is the branch point between primary isoprenoid metabolism (sterols for membranes, brassinosteroids for growth, ubiquinone for respiration, protein prenylation for signaling) and specialized defense metabolism (sesquiterpenes like beta-caryophyllene). Every molecule of FPP diverted to beta-caryophyllene synthesis is unavailable for sterol/brassinosteroid/GA biosynthesis.
- **Metabolic burden:** In microbial engineering studies, beta-caryophyllene production from glucose has a conversion efficiency of ~1.69% (1.52 g/L in E. coli fed-batch), indicating significant carbon/energy investment per molecule
- **MVA pathway requirement:** Beta-caryophyllene synthesis requires 3 acetyl-CoA + 3 NADPH + 3 ATP per FPP molecule (via MVA pathway), plus the TPS catalysis step
- **Defense cost in context:** During germination, diverting FPP toward sesquiterpene volatiles represents a direct metabolic trade-off against sterol biosynthesis (needed for membrane expansion in rapidly dividing cells) and brassinosteroid/GA production (needed for growth signaling)

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:** Research question 3 — "What is the causal chain from suppressing... beta-caryophyllene synthases... to freeing metabolic resources for growth?" This paper establishes that beta-caryophyllene synthase (TPS1) consumes FPP, the same precursor needed for sterol, brassinosteroid, and GA biosynthesis. Downregulating this enzyme would redirect FPP flux toward growth-promoting pathways.
- **Partially addresses:** Research question 7 (Dominant Theme Synthesis) — defense-to-growth reallocation via metabolic precursor competition
- **Novel contribution:** Provides the biochemical basis for understanding how beta-caryophyllene synthase downregulation could free FPP for primary metabolism. The key insight is that FPP is a shared precursor at the MVA pathway branch point, and TPS enzymes like GhTPS1 are JA-inducible defense genes. Suppressing their expression during germination would constitute a metabolic resource reallocation from defense volatiles to membrane/hormone biosynthesis.
