# Reduced V-ATPase Activity in the trans-Golgi Network Causes Oxylipin-Dependent Hypocotyl Growth Inhibition in Arabidopsis

- **Year:** 2008

## Abstract
Regulated cell expansion allows plants to adapt their morphogenesis to prevailing environmental conditions. Cell expansion is driven by turgor pressure created by osmotic water uptake and is restricted by the extensibility of the cell wall. The V-ATPase could provide a way to coordinately regulate turgor pressure and cell wall synthesis, as it energizes the secondary active transport of solutes across the tonoplast and also has an important function in the trans-Golgi network (TGN), which affects synthesis and trafficking of cell wall components. The det3 mutant, with reduced V-ATPase activity, has a severe defect in cell expansion. V-ATPase activity in the TGN was found to be necessary for cell expansion, linking vacuolar proton pumping with membrane trafficking and cell wall biosynthesis.

### Key Findings
- V-ATPase has a dual role: (1) energizing solute transport across the tonoplast for turgor pressure, and (2) acidifying the trans-Golgi network (TGN) for membrane trafficking and cell wall component synthesis/secretion
- The det3 mutant (reduced V-ATPase activity) has a severe cell expansion defect — specifically reduced hypocotyl elongation
- V-ATPase activity in the TGN is necessary for normal cell expansion
- The cell expansion defect in det3 is oxylipin-dependent — reduced V-ATPase triggers oxylipin biosynthesis that inhibits growth
- Cell expansion is driven by turgor pressure (osmotic water uptake) and constrained by cell wall extensibility
- V-ATPase provides a mechanism to coordinately regulate both turgor pressure AND cell wall synthesis, since it functions in both tonoplast and TGN

### Relevance to Research Brief
- **Directly addresses:** Research Question 2 — V-ATPase H role in vacuolar acidification and turgor-driven cell expansion. This paper demonstrates that V-ATPase activity is essential for cell expansion, operating through both turgor generation (tonoplast) and membrane trafficking (TGN). If xRNA downregulates V-ATPase H subunit, this could reduce both turgor pressure and secretory pathway function.
- **Partially addresses:** The dual TGN/tonoplast role of V-ATPase connects to the broader membrane trafficking network involving ROP GTPases (paper_027), D6PK/PIN recycling (papers_028/029), and SCAMP vesicle trafficking (paper_066). V-ATPase TGN acidification is upstream of all these trafficking events.
- **Novel contribution:** The oxylipin connection is unexpected and important. If V-ATPase H downregulation by xRNA triggers oxylipin accumulation, this could activate jasmonate/oxylipin defense signaling, potentially COUNTERACTING the defense-to-growth reallocation theme (Research Question 3). Alternatively, if the xRNA simultaneously downregulates defense genes (RPP13-like, etc.), the oxylipin-mediated growth inhibition might be buffered. This creates a complex interaction between the membrane trafficking and defense reallocation modules.
